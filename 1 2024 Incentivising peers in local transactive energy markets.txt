Abstract
A decarbonised future grid should couple technological novelty with innovative market
models to efficiently capture the value of grid‐edge decarbonised assets. The transactive
energy (TE) concept inverts the centralised grid model by leveraging the evolution of
consumers to prosumers to prosumagers. The principal TE market design challenge is
transactive control—using market and pricing mechanisms to coordinate autonomous
peer interactions, to optimally allocate power and incentivise peers. Peer attraction,
incentivisation and retention are all critical for practical TE implementation along three
adoption stages, starting from independent peer transactions with the centralised market;
to decentralised peer coordination; towards distributed peer‐to‐peer trading. Addressing
gaps in related scholarship, the authors investigate the economic positions of distinct peer
roles in each adoption stage and two local pricing strategies. Using a real market dataset,
trading decisions are simulated over a 1‐year horizon at hourly granularity. Coordinated
action achieves better transactive control for the community, with economic superiority
over centralised and distributed mechanisms. Distinct peer incentives should equitably
align with their contribution to market functionality, such as the value ascribed to prosumagers' flexibility in local pricing and the constrained bargaining power of prosumers
in distributed bilateral negotiations.

1 | INTRODUCTION
1.1 | Motivation
Legacy grids typically transmit unidirectional power flows from
predominantly huge, high carbon emitting generators to passive
end users. Carbon neutrality policy motivation and technological innovation have facilitated the proliferation of smaller scale,
distributed low carbon generation and multidirectional power
flows. Intermittent decarbonised power makes power quality
control and local supply‐demand balancing difficult for network
operators, leading to their managed curtailment in favour of
high carbon, dispatchable generation.
The future grid can be envisioned to be optimally efficient,
economical, ecological and equitable. This vision can be

-

facilitated via smart grid technologies to intelligently monitor,
capture, control and integrate the power, information and
economic actions of all network participants [1, 2]. This system
level innovation should couple business model innovation to
fully engage the adapting roles of all market participants as the
existing centralised market model fails to effectively capture an
increasingly localised energy value. Several local, microgrid or
community‐based energy market archetypes have been proposed [3, 4].
The power market exists to principally create energy value
for and capture economic value from the grid edge, where the
shift from passive to active market involvement is accelerating.
Business models that aspire to disruptively innovate the
incumbent should effectively address the motivations of
empowered consumers and prosumers. Beneficially, prosumers

provision of physical or virtual local, microgrid or macrogrid

Inverting the current grid logic by utilising prosumers for
decentralised generation and supply‐demand balancing is
termed transactive energy (TE) [7]. Transactive energy is
defined as “a system of economic and control mechanisms that
allows the dynamic balance of supply and demand across the
entire electrical infrastructure using value as a key operational
parameter” [8]. Local energy markets which provide marketplaces for the exchange of locally generated power are recognised as a route to realising fully TE systems [9]. Indeed, the
smart grid future can be built from a nexus of smart microgrids
and local energy marketplaces. The past decade has seen an
increase in research on local energy market (LEM), peer‐to‐
peer (P2P) energy trading and transactive energy market
(TEM). A comprehensive compilation is in ref. [10].
SESAM (Self Organisation and Spontaneity in Liberalised
and Harmonised Markets) is one early pilot of a P2P market
platform, executed with electronic contracts to transparently
integrate peer autonomy, spontaneity and incentivisation
through decentralised control mechanisms and secure
communication [11, 12]. The Brooklyn Microgrid project has
emerged as a prototypical, cyber‐physical TE market with
extensive referencing in related literature since 2017, fronted by
Mengelkamp et al. [13] who contextualised a 7‐component
architecture. The physical layer includes the grid connection,
market set‐up (peer composition and energy profiling) and
energy management systems (for supply‐demand monitoring,
order submissions and fulfilment), interfacing with a virtual
layer comprising the information system (platform for secure
transactions) which executes the market mechanism (bidding
rules for efficient power allocation) and pricing mechanism
(incentivising strategies within the market mechanism). Regulation is the seventh component.
Thus, subject to the extrinsic regulatory enablers, a transactive market must (i) intrinsically coordinate the actions and
satisfy the motivations of autonomous peers through an efficient market and pricing mechanism within (ii) a secure communications platform.
What are the motivations for peer participation and how
can these be met?
A survey conducted on participants in the Centrica‐
Cornwall LEM pilot involving 100 households where 54
were provided with free solar PV and all with free battery installations indicates that the top 3 motivating factors for
joining the LEM were wanting a clean energy supply, reducing
carbon emissions and lowering bills [14]. However, only 4%
were prepared to join the LEM if they had to bear the capital
costs, with respondents expecting a discount and guaranteed
reduction in electricity bill on any investment. The free text‐
box question of “What do you think would need to change

673

to encourage other households to participate in a local energy
market?” received the most responses of 84, with the financial
factor sighted as both a barrier and incentive. 63% preferred an
automated trading route, tuned to their distinct preferences. A
survey of 261 German households on their motivation for
LEM participation suggested a strong importance in security of
supply, although the economic and ecological benefits were
underlying security‐of‐supply drivers [15]. Having distinct local
energy pricing is reported to be a ‘high value booster’ for the
EMPOWER LEM project [16].
Answering the motivation question to align the right incentives requires recognising the distinct peer characterisations
in a transactive market set‐up. Presently, three peer groups exist
—consumers, prosumers and prosumagers [17]. Consumers
purely have a demand profile, prosumers both consume and
generate power, whereas prosumagers are prosumers with
energy storage. Transactive energy market design should follow
the principles of electricity market design where rules are
defined to optimally allocate power subject to network constraints [18] with the distinct economic objectives of peers
incentivised to drive an efficient competitive market [19].
Effectively matching locally generated power to local demand
can enable primary substations to maintain their regulated
power limits without additional constraint management.

1.2 | Scope and contribution
Whereas the theme of TE market design has attracted significant research recently, the increasing trend in algorithmic sophistication should not shift the focus from peer‐centric
analysis. Computationally tested TEM designs extensively
quantify the collective community benefit, rather than the
differentiated incentives of each peer characteristic. Though a
sense of community can galvanise peer participation, the decision to participate is an individual one. Thus, the expected
incentives should rationally align with the level of investment –
ergo, prosumagers may expect to be better incentivised than
prosumers and then consumers.
Our identification of the scalability issue in computational
analyses is supported by Capper et al. [10], who highlighted that
96 out of 119 papers case studied markets comprising up to 100
participants. Further gaps exist in terms of simulating diverse
peer profiles within a realistic market size, over a longer time
horizon. By investigating 300 prosumers with heterogeneous
portfolios, Ye at al. [20] attempt to address the diversity issue,
although consumers were excluded, and test cases covered 52
randomly selected days from an annual dataset. In addition to
peer evolution, the three market design phases towards TEM
maturity should be juxtaposed to shape the strategy for practical
deployment. This market evolution was examined by Guerrero
et al. [21] for a community of 30 consumers, 20 prosumers and
50 prosumagers, albeit over a 24‐h trading simulation.
To address these gaps, this paper is structured as follows:
Section 2 outlines the broad market design principles along
the TE maturity roadmap. The mathematical frameworks
governing their economic evaluation are formulated.

2 | TRANSACTIVE ENERGY MARKET
DESIGN
As per definition, a TE market consists of economic and energy control mechanisms—known as transactive control [8]
which organises peer interaction through economic signals to
optimise power allocation [25].
TEMs, like other market‐based contracts, are underpinned
by the fundamental economic rules of individual peer rationality (IR), incentive compatibility (IC) and utility preservation (UP) [26]. The TEM design aim is to coordinate peers'
behaviours to maximise overall system efficiency or social
welfare, subject to these rules [18]. A peer has a voluntary
willingness to participate in the market and exhibits a rational
behaviour (IR) by taking decisions and actions which maximise
its personal objective. A peer is duly incentivised to actively
participate in the market (IC), and to a level of satisfaction that
retains their participation (UP).
Peers use energy management devices (intelligent agents)
to automatically communicate and negotiate their actions with
each other for effective dispatch and supply‐demand balancing
through electronic market algorithms [9, 27]. Transactive
control techniques will evolve along the industrial adoption
stages identified in ref. [8]. This trajectory to TE maturity
initiates from centralised, develops to decentralised, towards
fully distributed mechanisms.
� Independent Peer Strategy (IPS), the first mechanism, is
the status‐quo, independent peer‐to‐grid model, described
as self‐optimisation in ref. [8] and uncoordinated approaches in ref. [21]. Peers transact independently with the
centralised grid via their retailer with whom they exchange
power and economic information (Figure 1(i)). In each
trading period, peers autonomously manage their energy,
subject to the grid import and export price signals, to
minimise their bill, based on the formulation in Section 2.1.
The IPS is the baseline against which the other adoptive
mechanisms are measured. Self‐optimisation approaches
were applied in refs. [20, 21, 28, 29] to model the strategic
trading decisions of independent market agents.
� Local Energy Sharing (LES) defines the second adoption
stage, a decentralised, coordinated [21] mechanism. A local
market operator acts as an intermediary between peers and
the retailer, acquiring information on the demand and
generation profiles and storage assets within the community
(Figure 1(ii)). Acting on behalf of participants, the operator's
objective is to maximise social welfare or minimise the
aggregate community bill. It executes this by using the
external price signals to coordinate the efficient allocation of
power locally to prioritise local supply‐demand balance and

-

675

minimise grid imports. The computational formulation is
outlined in Section 2.2. LES approaches have been
computationally examined by the authors in refs. [20, 21, 29,
30]. LES facilitates the adoption of local pricing strategies
(Section 2.2.1). Once the aggregate bill is settled with the
grid, the pro‐rated economic burden or revenue can be
allocated to each peer based on their distinct demand or
supply contribution.
� Distributed Peer‐to‐Peer (P2P) – with no intermediary,
distributed communications at each peer node enable the
direct bilateral exchange between a seller [a peer with surplus
power] and buyer [a peer with deficit] in discretised trading
slots. Unlike the decentralised approach, peers have autonomy
over their information and self‐optimise their energy
schedule, to execute smart contracts (a set of algorithmically
written protocols between peers [31]). Distributed ledger
technology, such as blockchain is characteristically suited to
provide a platform with non‐discriminatory peer access for
transparent, secure, non‐repudiable P2P exchanges as highlighted in Section 1.1. P2P mechanisms have been categorised
into optimisation methods, game theory, matching algorithms
and auctions [21, 32–34]. P2P exchanges, in the pure sense, are
of a bilateral nature with full peer autonomy, executable
through matching algorithm or auctions. Game theory is
limited to theoretical simulations as in practice, peers may not
have information on each other [34] unless in the LES.
Whereas LES models result in energy and economic transfer
from one peer to the other, they are not a P2P model, as
transactive control lies with the local operator. Indeed, whilst
an operator may manage the information system that facilitates P2P transactions, energy management autonomy resides
with individual peers.
Auction‐based methods are a zero‐intelligence approach. An
auction algorithm was originally proposed [35] for a distributed resource allocation problem with limited local information on network data and topology. A double auction (DA)
consists of multiple buyers and sellers where both parties
submit bids or asks in allocated time slots. A variant is the
continuous double auction characterised by continual market
clearing in the auction period [36, 37]. Double auctions are
used in commodities trading and stock exchange. Buyers
submit bid orders of [quantity, price] and sellers submit paired
ask orders. Bid orders are sorted in descending order and ask
orders in ascending order, the highest bid price is then
matched with the lowest ask price [38, 39]. In this paper, a DA
mechanism is formulated in Section 2.3 to execute distributed
P2P trades, drawing on related work in ref. [40]. Distributed
approaches have scalability advantages. The absence of
cooperative action via a local coordinator may yield inefficient
resource allocation, resulting in social welfare not being
maximised for the community. Another challenge is that
network operators may lack the visibility of P2P trading for
efficient operational management. In ref. [41], a P2P trading
with network constraint modelling is used to assess trading
impacts on a typical UK low‐voltage network. To facilitate
upstream‐downstream energy balancing, Morstyn et al. [42]
designed real‐time and forward contracts to allow bilateral

An internal pricing scheme can be adopted within the LEM to
reallocate the aggregate LES bill to peers. This is a potential
win‐win for both retailer and community as the retailer preserves the aggregate economic value captured from the community and the community can assign to peers an economic
reward or burden based on their contribution to the discretised
local supply‐demand status. Several local pricing strategies are
proposed in literature, such as bill sharing, mid‐market rate
(MMR) [23] and pricing based on the supply‐demand ratio
(SDR) [44]. The bill sharing model is however suboptimal for
prosuming peers as it does not incentivise them fairly for their
local generation and flexibility, making them worse‐off than in
the IPS, [22, 23] at the benefit of consumers who receive local
power at a discount. MMR was used to encourage coalitional
game theoretic prosumer exchanges [45] and analyse the
trading decisions among prosumers with multiple generation
and demand portfolios [30]. SDR was demonstrated in the
energy sharing among peers in community microgrids [28, 46].

3 | CASE STUDY
The aim is to examine the economic outcomes for the three
evolutionary peer types (consumers, prosumers, prosumagers)
when they participate in the five market designs (three mechanisms and two pricing strategies) along the TE maturity
roadmap. The mathematical formulations outlined in Sections 2.1, 2.2, MMR pricing, SDR pricing and 2.3 for the
market designs were computationally simulated using the
market construct and input data described in Section 3.1.
For every daily trading period with hourly timesteps,
T ¼ f1; 2; 3; :::; 24g, the IPS and LES cost minimisation
models were algorithmically written and individually solved
using a Python‐based optimisation tool on an Intel(R) Core™
i7‐9850H, 2.6 GHz processor, 32 GB RAM computer. For the
MMR and SDR pricing models, the respective costs are
calculated as in Equations (22) and (38), with local buy and sell
prices derived from the discretised flexible net demand values
in the LES optimisation output. The distributed peer‐to‐peer
exchanges in the DA are executed in the bilateral matching
algorithm outlined in Section 2.3. In the DA, the amount of
surplus electricity offered for sale by a prosumager is based on
its optimised flexible net demand output in the IPS, whereas
prosumers and consumers trade based on their baseline net
demand values.
In Section 3.2.2, the quantified community bill and the
differentiated bill for each peer group are extracted from the
simulation output.

Economic analysis under distributed P2P DA
In the P2P DA, sellers (peers with a surplus net demand)
autonomously ask prices which are randomly selected between
the FiT and ToU. Buyers (consuming peers) negotiate buy
prices bilaterally with individual sellers. Within the FiT to ToU
range constraint, a correlation is seen between the P2P
auctioned prices (Figure 20) and the intra‐day supply‐demand
variability (Figure 9). Aside a few peak and off‐peak time
outliers, P2P trading typically occurs between 08:00–18:00
(Figure 21) during which prosumers' restricted bargaining
power compels them to sell their excess generation at the lower
exchange prices. They sell an aggregate 216 MWh annually to
the community, export an additional 53 MWh to the grid at the
lower FiT and import 816 MWh to fulfil their deficit net demand at the higher ToU. Prosumers' yearly baseline deficit and

� Consumers:
The mechanism of pure LES without a local pricing
strategy may not attract the participation of consumers if
their local power purchases are priced at the ToU,
making them not economically better off than buying
from their retailer. By introducing local pricing, consumers can be given a discount on their local purchases
from the savings accrued by the community through
the LES.
� Prosumers:
Prosumers' generation supports market models which aim
to match local generation to demand. For solar prosumers
however, their peculiar generation profiles limit their independent bargaining power in bilateral P2P negotiations.
Their economic outcomes improve when they relinquish
their autonomy to a local operator to maximise their generation assets in the LES.
� Prosumagers:
Prosumagers' flexibility is a critical asset for LES coordination and a significant contributor to the community's bill
reduction. This must be duly incentivised through a more
equitable local pricing strategy such as the MMR, since the
SDR devalues the flexibility dispatched by prosumagers to
cover peak demand, at the benefit of consuming peers.
With full autonomy, prosumagers can push their negotiating position to capture more economic value through
P2P transactions. Their strategy should be community‐
tailored by juxtaposing the economic outcome of their
P2P autonomy with that which a locally priced LES
mechanism could offer.
� Future direction:
Contrary to refs. [15, 23, 48] and similar propositions, the
level of PV penetration and consumer‐to‐prosumer ratios
are not the critical success criteria for locally driven TE
markets. Local flexibility is the key enabler for optimal
power allocation, subject to external price signals.
Currently, grid‐edge participants lack full visibility of the
centralised real‐time price signals. This, together with the
absence of regulatory enablers, is the barrier to the
practical implementation of local TE markets. The
extension to this work is the execution of P2P transactions via smart contracts on a distributed platform (to
address the second intrinsic TE market design challenge)
and signposting the required legislative drivers (the
extrinsic TE market design challenge) for practical
deployment.
