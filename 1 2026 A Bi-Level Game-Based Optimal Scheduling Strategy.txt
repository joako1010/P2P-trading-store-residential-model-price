Abstract—To coordinate resource allocation among multi-level
and multi-agent stakeholders at local energy markets and facilitate the integration of shared energy storage, this paper proposes a Bi-Level Game-Based Optimal Scheduling Strategy for
Shared Energy Storage and Integrated Energy Microgrid (MG)
Clusters. Firstly, to address the disadvantaged position of shared
energy storage at local markets, a price-driven energy leasing
clearing mechanism is designed to enhance its market bargaining
power. Secondly, a Bi-Level optimization framework based on a
hybrid Nash–Stackelberg–Cooperative (N-S-C) game is developed,
of which, the upper level, the profit-maximization objectives of
microgrid operators (MOs) and shared energy storage operators (SESOs) are coordinated using Nash equilibrium theory to
achieve a dynamic balance of market power, the lower level, a
multi-stage decision-making mechanism is employed to minimize
the operational cost of MG coalition, improve renewable energy
utilization efficiency. Finally, a two-stage distributed optimization
framework is constructed to solve the proposed model, of which,

I. INTRODUCTION
N RECENT years, the rapid development of renewable
energy sources, such as wind and solar power, has profoundly reshaped the global energy landscape. This shift has
disrupted the traditional balance of electricity supply and demand, increased power flow fluctuations across transmission and
distribution networks, and posed significant challenges to grid
stability. In response, governments worldwide have promoted
the establishment of energy communities to support localized
energy generation and consumption [1].
Microgrid is a localized power generation and distribution system that integrates loads, distributed energy resources
(DERs) [2], energy storage systems, and energy conversion
technologies. Due to their strong self-balancing capability, microgrids (MGs) are considered as an effective solution for integrating distributed energy into the power system [3]. Numerous
studies have focused on MG energy management [4]. For example, [5] formulated a mixed-integer linear programming model
to optimize generation capacity and scheduling in multi-energy
MGs, considering electricity, heating, and transportation electrification. In [6], a multi-energy MG was modeled as a virtual
power plant providing ancillary services, where an integrated
demand response strategy was developed to mitigate conflicts of
interest between suppliers and consumers by exploiting demandside flexibility. Furthermore, [7] introduced a multi-objective
optimization method for critical load vector scheduling in MGs,
aiming to balance availability, net cost, and efficiency.
However, due to limited capacity and inertia, individual MGs
still face challenges in accommodating local renewable generation. To overcome these limitations, recent research has emphasized the coordinated operation of multiple interconnected
MGs—linking adjacent AC and DC MGs to exchange energy

and enhance overall system efficiency [8]. For instance, [9]
proposed a multi-tier market framework involving transmission
system operators, distribution system operators (DSOs), and
multiple MGs, wherein the DSO acts as a coordinator solving a
bilevel optimization problem using Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT)
conditions. Although this approach improves computational
efficiency, it compromises user privacy. To address this, [10]
introduced a peer-to-peer (P2P) low-carbon economic operation model for multi-microgrid (MMG) systems, employing
the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) to
decompose the problem into subproblems, thereby ensuring
both solution accuracy and privacy. Building on this, [11] introduced an enhanced ADMM algorithm incorporating a dynamic
penalty factor that adapts to the relationship between primal
and dual residuals, mitigating convergence instability due to
poor parameter initialization. Owing to its decentralized and
privacy-preserving characteristics, ADMM has become a widely
used method in MMG coordination.
Energy storage also plays a critical role in modern power
systems [12]. To address the spatiotemporal mismatch between
generation and demand, storage systems have been deployed
in both islanded and grid-connected MGs [13]. However, installing dedicated storage for individual MGs often leads to low
utilization and high costs. As a solution, the concept of shared
energy storage has gained attention and is increasingly applied
in MMG optimization [14]. For example, [15] evaluates the
economic and operational advantages of shared energy storage
under demand response, formulating an optimization model to
derive optimal strategies for both individual and shared systems.
Results show that shared storage notably reduces electricity
costs and improves utilization. Building on this, [16] presents
a storage-leasing business model for multiple industrial parks
and develops an iterative bi-level optimization framework to
determine optimal storage allocation. Moreover, [17] proposes
a multi-objective model for MGs to maximize power fluctuation mitigation and minimize storage costs, based on which
charge/discharge demands are obtained to guide storage leasing
decisions.
Despite these advances, most existing shared storage models
rely on fixed leasing fees, lacking the flexibility in pricing and
trading mechanisms needed to adapt to dynamic market conditions. This rigidity may limit competitiveness and discourage
long-term participation. Therefore, innovative market-clearing
mechanisms are urgently needed to enhance adaptability, participation, and economic viability.
In energy trading strategies involving multiple stakeholders,
achieving balanced and coordinated interest allocation remains a
core challenge. Game theory, as a fundamental tool for analyzing
strategic interactions and equilibrium outcomes among rational
agents, has been extensively applied in recent energy trading
studies [18]. For example, [19] proposed a non-cooperative
game-based optimization model for islanded microgrid clusters,
analyzing their planning, operation, and trading behaviors. In
[20], a cooperative game model was developed to enhance
synergy among MGs, incorporating a benefit allocation mechanism based on individual contributions and renewable energy
absorption ratios. In [21], a Stackelberg game model captured

the sequential decision-making dynamics between the DSO and

While these studies effectively model agent interactions, most
adopt a single-layer game-theoretic structure, which may be
inadequate for representing the complex strategic relationships
inherent in multi-level, multi-agent systems. To address this, [22]
introduced a hybrid game-theoretic strategy for shared energy
storage systems, combining a Stackelberg game between the
shared energy storage operator (SESO) and multiple microgrids
(MMGs) with a generalized Nash bargaining model incorporating compensation among MMGs. Similarly, [23] proposed
a two-layer framework embedding a cooperative game among
integrated energy management communities within a vertical
Stackelberg game involving the distribution network operator.
However, existing hybrid models primarily focus on embedding horizontal games among lower-level agents within a leader–
follower structure. The strategic interactions among upper-level
decision-makers—such as between microgrid operators (MOs)
and SESOs in pricing and resource allocation—remain insufficiently explored. To more accurately represent the strategic
landscape of local energy markets with hierarchical and crosslevel interactions, a more comprehensive hybrid game-theoretic
framework is needed. This framework should integrate both
vertical leader–follower dynamics and horizontal interactions
within and across hierarchical levels to overcome the structural
and modeling limitations of existing approaches.
Based on an in-depth literature review, this paper proposes a
hybrid bilevel game model involving MOs, SESOs, and multiple
interconnected MGs. The model holistically incorporates stakeholder interdependencies and sequential decision-making, addressing the limitations of existing game-theoretic frameworks
in capturing the structural complexity and modeling accuracy
required for multi-agent, multi-level decision scenarios. A twostage lower-level game is formulated based on energy scheduling
priorities, and a tailored distributed algorithm is developed to
solve it. The proposed model enhances stakeholder participation, achieves balanced interest distribution, and promotes the
development of regional energy markets and local renewable
energy consumption.

II. MMG SYSTEM MODEL WITH SHARED ENERGY STORAGE
A. Local Energy Market Framework
The local energy market is an innovative model for electricity trading and management that enhances system flexibility
through localized energy generation and consumption. This
study focuses on a community-level distributed energy market, where the main participants include MG users, SESOs,
and MOs. In this market, MG users act as energy consumers,

acquiring services primarily from SESOs and MOs. The overall
market structure and energy flow are illustrated in Fig. 1.
Functioning as an energy hub, the MO serves as an interface between multiple MGs and external energy networks. As
depicted in the figure, the MO procures electricity and natural
gas from the grid and gas network, respectively. Natural gas
is converted into electricity and heat using combined heat and
power (CHP) systems and gas boilers. The MO then supplies
this energy to MG users at specified prices, ensuring energy
provision for the cluster while generating revenue through
trading.
This paper proposes a market mechanism where the SESO
uses an independent clearing process based on energy leasing.
SESO determines leasing prices according to real-time market
conditions and operating costs, then sends price signals to microgrid clusters. In response, MGs develop leasing strategies
based on their load demands. Upon agreement, energy is physically transferred via power interconnections, enabling efficient
allocation and utilization of storage resources.
Each integrated energy MG consists of electrical and thermal
loads, DERs such as photovoltaic (PV) and wind power (WP),
and power-to-heat conversion devices. Through coordinated
management of self-generation and external supply, the MG
maintains internal source-load balance. Within the proposed
framework, regional MGs are interconnected via power lines
to facilitate P2P energy trading. Users first trade energy with
neighboring MGs; if a deficit remains, energy is acquired sequentially from the SESO and then the MO.

III. UNIFIED MODELING FRAMEWORK FOR N-S-C GAMES AND
ITS SOLUTION ALGORITHM FRAMEWORK.
A. Formulation of the Game Model and Proof of Equilibrium
Solution
1) Establishment of N-S-C Mixed Game Model: The proposed distributed energy trading market model must consider
both strategic coupling among multiple agents and temporal
dependencies inherent in multi-stage decision-making. In such
a complex game-theoretic setting, a single game model cannot
fully capture the intricate interactions. Nash and cooperative
games effectively model competitive or collaborative relationships among agents at the same decision level but cannot
handle hierarchical multi-stage decision processes. Conversely,
Stackelberg games capture sequential decisions in hierarchical
structures but overlook interactions among agents with equal
priority. Hence, developing a unified framework that integrates
cross-layer decision dependencies and intra-layer strategic interactions is crucial for complex energy trading systems.
To address this, a hybrid N–S–C game model is proposed,
combining horizontal and vertical game structures. The horizontal layer captures competition and cooperation among agents
at the same level, while the vertical layer reflects inter-level
decision dependencies. Through dynamic recursive equilibrium
analysis, the model reveals strategy propagation and benefit
allocation across multiple stages, offering theoretical insights
and practical guidance for energy markets.
As shown in Fig. 2, the bilevel game model consists of an
upper level with MOs and SESOs. Both maximize profits by
setting production plans and pricing strategies. Initially, operators determine energy prices based on costs and communicate
them to the lower level.

The lower level includes multiple independent MGs aiming
to minimize operational costs. MG users plan energy purchases,
load adjustments (including electric heating and demand response), and P2P trading. The lower-level optimization occurs
in two stages: first, users prioritize local energy consumption by
conducting P2P trades, with the market coordinator adjusting
prices iteratively based on supply–demand balance until equilibrium is reached; second, after finalizing P2P trades, users
develop energy purchasing and load adjustment plans informed
by upper-level prices and report these back. The upper level
updates revenues and prices accordingly. This iterative process
continues until no participant can improve profit unilaterally,
achieving a stable game equilibrium.
In the proposed game model, the vertical dimension reflects a
Stackelberg game, where upper-level pricing decisions precede
lower-level energy consumption responses. Horizontally, MGs
and SESOs compete for limited market share by strategically
setting energy service prices, forming a Nash game. Additionally, at the lower level, MGs establish profit-sharing coalitions
coordinated by a market agent who manages price adjustments
and energy exchanges to minimize system-wide costs. Profits
are distributed based on individual contributions, constituting a
cooperative game [23].
In summary, this paper develops a hierarchical N–S–C hybrid
game framework that integrates vertical Stackelberg dynamics
with horizontal Nash and cooperative interactions.

3) Shapley Value Allocation Method: Once the total revenue
of the lower-level MG cooperative coalition is determined, a
fair profit allocation method is required to ensure equitable
distribution among participating entities. The Shapley value is

a widely used approach for this purpose, as it quantifies each
entity’s marginal contribution to the total revenue across all
possible sub-coalition structures. Specifically, it computes the
average marginal contribution of an entity when added to every

1) Analysis of Optimal Pricing Strategies for MO and SESO:
The optimal electricity pricing strategies at the upper level are

the MO’s sale price and the grid feed-in tariff. This pricing

its economic viability.

V. CONCLUSION
This paper addresses multi-stakeholder coordination in a local
energy market and proposes an optimal scheduling strategy for
shared energy storage and integrated energy MG clusters based
on a hybrid game-theoretic model. The strategy accounts for
the interests of multiple hierarchical stakeholders, aiming to
enhance the overall economic benefits of market participants.
The main findings are summarized as follows:
1) The proposed N-S-C hybrid game framework effectively
characterizes the multi-stage, multi-agent game interactions in local energy markets and protects the interests of
all participants.
2) The proposed two-stage algorithm ensures hierarchical
energy scheduling, thereby promoting local consumption
of renewable energy.

1897

3) Compared to the fixed leasing fee model, the proposed price-driven SESO independent clearing model improves SESO’s revenue by 52.5% under the same conditions. Meanwhile, the proposed S-ADMM distributed
optimization algorithm guarantees global physical constraints for energy storage while preserving the privacy of
MG data from SESO.
4) A P2P trading mechanism for MG clusters is also developed using the E-ADMM algorithm and Shapley valuebased profit allocation. This approach maintains power
exchange balance and preserves data privacy across nodes.
Simulation results show that, compared to the no-P2P
scenario, the proposed method reduces MG operating
costs by 3.36%, 3.97%, 8.44%, and 17.91%, respectively.
To further enhance the proposed operational method, future
research can explore the following directions:
1) Incorporating real-world load fluctuations to better capture dynamic energy consumption behaviors;
2) Considering the impact of energy storage degradation on
long-term system operation and planning;
3) Analyzing the sensitivity of cooperative alliances to strategic misreporting.
