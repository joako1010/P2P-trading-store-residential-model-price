Powering sustainability: Analyzing local energy market equilibrium with
energy storage and renewable resources in oligopolistic grids
Hassan Geramifar , Majid Shahabi *, Taghi Barforoushi
Faculty of Electrical and Computer Engineering, Babol Noshirvani University of Technology, Babol, 47148-71167, Iran

Financial constraints, economic risks, and uncertain investment returns have led to the emergence of new players
and local energy markets (LEMs) in distribution networks. However, the limited energy transmission capabilities
of distribution networks and their inability to accommodate numerous players create conditions conducive to
oligopolies, potentially impacting the performance of LEMs. This paper proposes a day-ahead planning frame­
work for LEMs to address the competitive environment within distribution networks hosting distributed energy
resources (DERs). The research focuses on achieving equilibrium among oligopolistic entities, including the
distribution system operator (DSO). Simulation results on a sample network with energy storage systems (ESSs),
renewable energy sources (RESs), conventional distributed generation, and demand response resources reveal
that despite limited capacity, these resources can exert market power, leading to price fluctuations and dimin­
ishing social welfare. Our findings suggest that regulators should encourage broader market participation rather
than solely increasing renewable capacity to promote LEM sustainability and efficiency. Additionally, given the
substantial disparity in profit distribution between monopolistic and competitive markets, regulators should
consider implementing measures to protect intermediary players like energy storage systems, whose return on
investment may be adversely affected by others' market power. This work provides valuable insights for poli­
cymakers involved in developing and regulating sustainable energy systems equipped with ESSs.

1. Introduction
1.1. Background and motivation
The main goal of the Paris Agreement in 2015 was to limit the rise in
global temperatures to below 2 degrees Celsius for the entire current
century. This was to be achieved through the use of renewable energy
sources and improved energy efficiency. It is possible to achieve this
goal from both a technical and theoretical standpoint, which will result
in enhanced environmental and economic sustainability. A crucial
aspect of this endeavor is the substantial transformation of current en­
ergy systems, transitioning towards renewable sources and reducing
reliance on fossil fuels. Some countries are presently embracing an
approach known as the energy transition [1]. Despite the implementa­
tion of numerous programs by different governments to achieve pollu­
tion reduction, the progress in this process has been gradual. If the
current policies and plans, which entail the continued utilization of
fossil fuel resources, persist, the carbon dioxide gas quota for this cen­
tury will be depleted in fewer than 20 years. Consequently, it is

imperative to explore various angles about the development of renew­
able energy sources and the enhancement of energy efficiency. The
central pillar of the energy transition plan revolves around the concept
of a decarbonized power system, necessitating innovative approaches in
power system planning, operation, electricity markets, and public reg­
ulations [2]. This necessitates attention across all stages of generation,
transmission, and distribution of energy.
In recent decades, decentralized energy sources have been increas­
ingly integrated into distribution networks, leading to ongoing changes
in economic frameworks for their involvement in energy provision.
Nevertheless, in the present wholesale electricity markets, there are
obstacles to the integration of these resources, including constraints in
accommodating, intricacies in their management, and uncertainties in
tariff determination, transaction costs, and more.
So far, there is not a unified definition of the LEM, resulting in
divergent viewpoints on its operational framework. Nonetheless, a
prevailing concept in most literature is that the fundamental purpose of
the LEM is to enable energy transactions at the local level, such as within
distribution networks or microgrids [3]. The emergence of LEM in recent
years has provided a solution to many challenges. These markets

level, bringing together the operator, owners of DER, and end-users in a
specific framework. This arrangement helps in achieving goals such as
ensuring stability, improving reliability, and enhancing access to elec­
trical energy. To accomplish these aims, it is essential for the regulatory
framework of the system to go beyond top-down approaches and
incorporate bottom-up strategies. This involves promoting increased

energy markets, it is fundamental that transactions yield benefits for all
involved parties, and the presence of multiple players fosters competi­
tion and a pursuit of optimal outcomes. The process of bargaining to
enhance the advantages derived from a power system's generation
portfolio is a complex endeavor. However, this process has predomi­
nantly satisfied the interests of prominent entities, thereby hindering the
ability of smaller and medium-sized generation companies to capitalize
on the advantages offered by a market model founded on perfect
competition [5].
It is important to acknowledge that the most effective strategy for
these players to maximize their profits within the local market is still
challenging. In broad terms, the behavior of market participants in
power systems can be categorized into two types: strategic proposals and
non-strategic proposals. Non-strategic bidding refers to the situation
where market players solely focus on resolving their planning issues,
without taking into account the variables of other players, and without
impacting the market settlement price. Conversely, a strategic proposal
entails a market participant's ability to exert influence on the market
settlement prices by possessing a certain level of control over market
behavior, thereby leading to a substantial increase in their earnings [6].
Strategic producers, employ a range of strategies to exert their influence,
resulting in the formation of a market with imperfect competition. These
tactics include intentionally raising prices, purposeful reducing energy
supply, exiting the market through the establishment of bilateral con­
tracts, exploiting regulatory restrictions such as congestion in trans­
mission and distribution lines and voltage fluctuations, as well as
intentionally disconnecting cheaper units owned by them.
As we move from simply integrating DERs to deploying LEM as a
structured energy transaction platform, we begin to see how the stra­
tegic behavior of these resources emerges as a critical factor in social

welfare. While these topics may seem disparate at first glance, they
provide a common thread and strong motivation for studying energy
price manipulation in the early-stage LEM. Hence, this study synthesizes
these concepts to offer a holistic perspective on the economic implica­
tions of the transition phase in distribution grids. Our approach ad­
dresses a significant gap in the existing literature, which has
predominantly examined these topics in isolation, thereby limiting the
potential for a comprehensive analysis. By integrating these key areas,
we aim to elucidate the complex interplay of factors influencing eco­
nomic outcomes during the transitional period in distribution grids.
1.2. Literature review
It is worth noting that the majority of imperfect competition studies
have predominantly concentrated on wholesale markets. Nevertheless,
despite the adverse impacts of market power on social welfare and the
equitable distribution of income, there is a notable gap in examining this
issue extensively at the LEM and distribution network levels. An
example of this can be found in the investigation carried out in [7],
where a bi-level model concentrating on the corporation that controls
strategic ESS in the wholesale market was outlined. This model was
employed to find the Stackelberg equilibrium and shows the utilization
of market power through the escalation of prices. In [8,9], the authors
developed a model to simulate the exert of market power by a strategic
ESS entity through the reduction of supply capacity in the market.
References [10–13] delve into the application of market power by
various stakeholders in the wholesale market, focusing on modeling the
constraints of the transmission network and its impact on diminishing
social welfare. An economic model commonly employed to analyze
markets characterized by imperfect competition is the Bertrand model
[14]. Authors in [14], employ the price offer mechanism to determine
the equilibrium point within the wholesale market of England and
Wales. Notably, the Bertrand model does not consider any restrictions
on the supply capacity of producers. In contrast, the Cournot model, as
referenced in [15–18], introduces the concept of presenting proposed
packages in terms of energy quantity rather than energy price. In com­
parison to the Bertrand model, the Cournot model yields reasonable
outcomes and aligns better with the structure of electricity markets by

2. Model setup
In essence, a distribution network, undergoing a shift from a con­
ventional state to a restructured and decarbonized state in the transition
phase, possesses a limited number of DERs. The capacity of each of these
sources can be substantial when compared to the peak load level,
thereby presenting an opportunity to exert market power in a localized
energy market. Additionally, in this emerging situation, it is necessary to
allocate a designated entity, commonly referred to as a market operator,
for controlling and monitoring all energy exchanges, while also ensuring
network security. Implementing such an environment is feasible through
the utilization of the Community-Based Market (CBM) structure. The
proposed structure of this market is visually represented in Fig. 1.
Local energy resources are recognized as key players and price
makers since they pose substantial capacity concerning the load level of
the distribution network. On the contrary, the DSO is viewed as a nonstrategic and price-taker entity in comparison to the wholesale mar­
ket, given the relatively small total demand of the distribution network
compared to the volume of transactions in the wholesale market.
However, the DSO in the CBM-based restructured environment aims to
maximize its profit and will adopt a strategic behavior towards the
consumers.
The main objective of the proposed setup is to develop an appro­
priate, effective, and precise framework for investigating concerns
related to market clearing and energy transactions in the distribution
network undergoing the transition phase. To achieve this goal, a
representative distribution network including a limited number of
strategic participants, all aiming to optimize their gains, will be thor­
oughly examined and assessed.

• What model is the most appropriate for analyzing LEM during
transition periods as they shift towards a hosting platform from the
perspective of a DSO?
• Can DERs in LEM cause a purposeful deviation in the optimal equi­
librium point and impact social welfare negatively?
• How are price caps and potential price spikes determined in LEM
incorporating strategically positioned DERs?
1.3. Contributions and paper organization
Based on the topics discussed about the specific condition of the
distribution system in the transition phase, the research innovations and
contributions will include:
• Proposing an oligopolistic framework based on the Cournot model
that is specifically tailored for analyzing the transition phase of earlystage LEM considering network constraints and from the perspective
of all players.
• Presenting a holistic methodology to evaluate the possible price caps
and their correlation with the economic viability of market partici­
pants in the early-stage LEM.

Within this section, the problem is characterized by a mathematical
model incorporating common equations from game theory. The under­
lying premise of the ongoing study is that strategic players will employ a
tactic of reducing energy supply intentionally to drive up prices [37].
The analysis of the electricity market with strategic players reveals that
the predominant frameworks are centered on three key game types:
Bertrand, Cournot, and Stackelberg. It has been previously emphasized
that the Cournot model is more appropriate for capturing the essence of
electricity markets compared to the Bertrand model. The Cournot model
assumes that the market power of various players is nearly equal, with
no player being superior to others when it comes to strategy selection. In
this model, all players make decisions simultaneously. This classification
highlights the diverse levels of market power in the decision-making
process. It is important to note that all three game categories
mentioned are non-cooperative games. The symbols utilized in the
mathematical model can be found in nomenclature.
The resolution of market clearing problems through the application
of game theory necessitates the evaluation of demand behavior and its
reaction to price fluctuations. This study employs the linear inverse
demand function, a widely accepted method in economic research [38].

3.4. DSO problem
Responsible for tasks assigned to a market operator during the
transition phase, the DSO pursues multiple goals. These objectives
include promoting consumer surplus, maximizing profit, ensuring
market clearing conditions, and supervising network technical re­
strictions. The objective function and constraints of this player are
presented in (17) to (28).
The objective function of DSO comprises five distinct terms as
modified social welfare (MSW). The initial term pertains to the surplus
of customers. The second term involves revenue generated from energy
sales, competing against other key players in the market. The remaining
parts, from three to five, encompass expenses related to energy pro­
curement from the wholesale market, losses, and load curtailment.

of producers' supply. Therefore, a set of constraints is necessary to link
the different problems of participants to find the equilibrium. These

3.6. Finding the equilibrium point
In markets where strategic players lack superiority or priority in
strategy selection, the equilibrium is reached by concurrently addressing
the problem of maximizing utility for all players, resembling the Cournot
equilibrium. It is important to highlight that within game theory,
Cournot's game outcomes yield the highest market clearing prices and
the least social welfare. This setting is perceived as a fiercely competitive
environment for exerting market power. To determine the Cournot
equilibrium among various participants in this study, the approach in­
volves replacing the optimization problem of each participant with their
respective optimality conditions. The process, as illustrated in Fig. 3,
applies the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions to address the issue. The
process of achieving equilibrium is well-known to be contingent upon
the simultaneous resolution of numerous equations. The objective

5.4. Policy and regulatory insights
This section explores potential regulatory pathways aimed at
fostering efficiency and providing sustainability and competitiveness in
LEMs. Following the detailed descriptions of three representative policy
mechanisms, a quantitative evaluation of their effectiveness on the
market performance is systematically performed through Case Study 6.
A. Policy 1: price regulation through market caps
The imposition of price caps on the local market equilibrium is a
well-established regulatory mechanism to prevent excessive price fluc­
tuations and to mitigate the potential exercise of market power. By
limiting the maximum clearing price, this policy ensures affordability
for consumers while maintaining a competitive balance among market
participants. This policy introduces a more realistic representation of
consumer behavior by effectively transforming the inverse demand
curve into a piecewise linear function. This adjustment reflects the
practical reality that consumers exhibit limited willingness to pay
beyond a certain threshold, regardless of system conditions. To apply
this strategy, a cap equal to 1.6 times the hourly wholesale market price
is considered, which allows a servicing fee of up to 60 % above the
wholesale price at the retail or local market level. Consequently, the
incorporation of capped demand not only constrains excessive market
prices but also enhances the ability of the model in capturing realistic
market dynamics.

C. Policy 3: constant daily energy consumption under RTP scheme
Keeping the total daily energy consumption constant and equal to the
perfect competition case, similar to Real-Time Pricing (RTP) schemes,
represents another approach to stabilize local market outcomes. By
constraining aggregate demand while allowing intra-day load shifting,
this mechanism limits the ability of strategic firms to exploit demand
elasticity, thereby reducing volatility in prices and enhancing social
welfare. This strategy is performed by the DSO and the corresponding
constraint is added to its mathematical model.

6. Conclusion
This paper introduces an effective framework for the comprehensive
analysis and investigation of the LEM, with a specific emphasis on the
transition phase of the distribution network from traditional to
restructured states. The research methodology employed in this study is
centered on the economic evaluation of various monopoly conditions
prevalent in LEM, and how these conditions impact the profitability of
the players present within the distribution network. In the proposed
framework for short-term day-ahead planning, multiple entities
including solar RES, ESS, small-scale DGs, demand response resources,
and price-sensitive loads are taken into account. The outcomes obtained
from this framework effectively depict the characteristics of a smart
distribution grid during the transition phase. The equilibrium point of
the problem is obtained using a strategy derived from the theory of noncooperative games. Furthermore, the proposed approach incorporates
