ABSTRACT
Integrated use of electricity and heat is an effective way to improve energy efficiency, precipitating the
advent of multi-energy systems. In a network of multi-energy systems connected via power electronics devices,
the power flow through soft-open points can be directly controlled, which allows peer-to-peer (P2P) energy
trading. This paper proposes an online distributed decision scheme for this scenario. We begin with the
deterministic centralized model of P2P energy trading problem, aiming to minimize long-term energy cost.
Then the problem is decomposed temporally and spatially. To handle uncertainty, Lyapunov optimization is
employed to decompose inter-temporal constraints in storage operation model, thus the problem in each period
can be solved independently, yielding an online prediction-free policy. It is proven that, by properly selecting
parameters, online policy generates a strategy that satisfies inter-temporal constraints and enjoys guaranteed
performance. To protect privacy, an alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) based algorithm is
employed to decompose the one-time-slot problem spatially, so that the subproblem of each peer admits a
solver-free solution. The P2P trading price is intrinsically generated during the consensus-reaching process.
Case studies demonstrate the prediction-free, performance-competitive, solver-free advantages of the proposed
method. Both total and individual energy costs are reduced, and close to offline optimum.

1. Introduction
The integration of heat, electricity, and gas can exploit the synergy
of different energy carriers [1–3]. For example, the waste heat during
electricity production can be collected and reused, thus improving
overall efficiency; heat is easier to store, providing more flexibility to
utilize volatile renewable energy. On this account, multi-energy system
plays an indispensable role in promoting the green and sustainable
development of renewable-based cities, which has been widely implemented in industrial park and residential community [4,5]. Energy hub
(EH) is a typical framework of multi-energy system [6–8]. It integrates
energy production, conversion, and storage facilities into a single plant.
Through efficient use of renewable energy, it becomes a promising
paradigm in the low-carbon society. Since the demand profiles of different consumers are somehow complementary, connecting energy hubs
via power electronics devices and allowing energy trading among them
can further improve operation flexibility and reduce the investment
in backup resources [9]. Peer-to-peer (P2P) trading/sharing is a new
market scheme, which allows a peer to buy∕sell energy from∕to the
neighboring peers instead of a grid utility [10–14]. This new market
scheme has attracted significant attention in recent years.

The existing researches on modeling the P2P market can be roughly
classified into two groups: centralized mode and decentralized mode.
For the centralized mode, a service provider can manage the energy
flow and set trading prices/allocate benefit [15]. In [16], a two-stage
aggregated battery control strategy is proposed to facilitate P2P energy
trading, where the rule-based control method is developed for realtime operation of energy storage devices. A stochastic leader-follower
game is proposed to describe the interaction between the P2P service
provider and prosumers; as the leader, the service provider adopts a
dynamic pricing scheme [17]. In [18], the transformer operator is set
as an aggregator to coordinate the P2P energy trading among smart
homes, where the P2P price is established based on the mid-market
rate. In such a mode, privacy protection and unbiased pricing or benefit
allocation are challenging tasks. Therefore, the decentrailized mode
is another popular framework. For the decentralized mode, aiming
to maximize individual profits, peers are given the freedom to make
self-interest decisions, and the market equilibrium emerges when the
amount of energy trading and corresponding prices reach a consensus [19–21]. In [22], a real-time energy trading platform based on a
decentralized digital currency technology is designed for prosumers;

The aforementioned literature primarily focuses on the electricity
sector, neglecting the complexity inherent in energy hub systems that
encompass a diverse array of energy carriers and sophisticated energy
conversion and storage technologies. This oversight is significant, as the
energy management of an energy hub necessitates a more complicated
approach due to the interactions between multiple energy forms and
devices. For instance, a rule-based control method can be used for
real-time battery operation in the electricity-only scenario, but such
simplistic control strategies are rendered infeasible in energy hub operation because of the integration of multi-carriers [16]. Furthermore,
this complexity is exacerbated in scenarios involving P2P energy exchange and management across multiple interconnected energy hubs,
necessitating the development of advanced methodologies to address
the multifaceted nature of energy management in such integrated
systems. There are some studies on the P2P transactive energy for
multiple energy hubs. In [25], a P2P transaction platform is established
for networked energy hubs to motivate energy trading and improve
social welfare. In [26], the cooperative game theory is used to design a
fair and efficient payoff allocation mechanism for P2P energy trading
among energy hubs with high penetration of renewable energy; the
cooperative game is proved to be balanced, ensuring the stability of
the grand coalition. In [27], a fully decentralized model based on the
alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM) is proposed to enable P2P energy trading among industrial, commercial, and residential

A common feature of the above works [25–30] is that they adopt a
deterministic optimization model with exact problem data to analysis
the market equilibrium and benefit of P2P trading, neglecting the
system uncertainty. However, in practical implementation, the system
uncertainties, including renewable generation, load profile, and energy
prices, have significant effects on energy hub operation economy and
reliability, and thus could not be ignored. Especially in real-time operation, the uncertainty is revealed period by period, and the dispatch
decisions in each period are also made after observing the uncertainty
realization; but the decisions made in the current period will affect
the feasible action set in future periods, thus affecting the economy
and reliability. Therefore, many approaches are developed to tackle
the uncertainty obstacle. Main approaches for handling uncertainties
include:
(i) Rolling horizon model predictive control (MPC) [31]. This approach solves the deterministic optimization problem with forecast
information in a rolling horizon manner. In the P2P trading market proposed in [32], the prosumers modify their scheduled power
plan in accordance with predicted information under the MPC framework. However, the performance largely depends on the forecast accuracy [33]. Since renewable power forecast error in a small area is
typically large [34], the performance of MPC is not always good.
(ii) SP/RO/DRO. Stochastic programming (SP) assumes knowing
the probability distribution of uncertainty and optimizing the expectation [35]. In practice, the probability distribution can be obtained
from historical data or Monte Carlo simulation [36]. However, the
highly accurate prediction of uncertainty distribution is hard. Robust
optimization (RO) builds the uncertainty set according to all possible
uncertain parameter values and optimizes the result under the worst
scenario [37]. RO is criticized for being overly conservative, especially
in the application of user-side energy management problems. Distributionally robust optimization (DRO) makes a good compromise between
SP and RO and the uncertainty is described by a set of probability
distributions around an empirical one [38]. SP/RO/DRO usually adopt
a two-stage framework, where the decision is made in the first stage,
and uncertainty is revealed in the second stage. They are widely applied
in planning problem [39–41] and scheduling problem [42–44]. However, in real-time decision-making problem, the uncertainty in each
period is observed sequentially and the decisions are made period-byperiod based on the realized uncertainty, which is a typical multistage
stochastic optimization problem. In this regard, classical SP/RO/DRO
are not suitable for solving such problems.
(iii) Dynamic programming method. Dynamic programming is a
classical paradigm for multistage optimization problems and can obtain
sequential decisions. This method can be roughly categorized into
model-based approaches and model-free approaches. For model-based
approaches, stochastic dual dynamic programming [45] and robust
dual dynamic programming [46] are two main approaches, where
the optimal value functions are piecewise linear and constructed via
forward and backward passes. The former employs the expectation of
scenario tree, while the latter considers the worst scenario. Recently,
DRO-based dynamic programming has also attracted attention [47,
48], which minimizes the worst-case expected cost over an ambiguity
set of probability distributions and makes decisions period-by-period.
However, all these approaches are computationally expensive when
the decision-making stage grows. For model-free approaches, reinforcement learning [49] is popular for approximately constructing optimal

value function and generating real-time decision strategies. Nevertheless, the learning approach requires adequate historical data, and the
convergence performance is sensitive to the quality of data.
(iv) Lyapunov optimization. Lyapunov optimization is a predictionfree queue scheduling method and is first proposed to solve the stochastic optimization problem in communication and queuing networks
[50]. It refers to introducing Lyapunov function to optimally control
a dynamic system with time-coupling queue constraints. In Lyapunov
optimization framework, it minimizes the long-term time-average cost
and the time-coupling constraint is replaced by queue stability condition; thus, the stochastic optimization is finally cast into a driftplus-penalty problem and can be solved sequentially without referring
to any future prediction information. Given the similarity between
energy storage and the accumulation of queues, it has been applied to
storage-related real-time operation problems. In [51], real-time energy
management of wind-storage integrated power plant is investigated,
where the real-time dispatch decisions are made based on Lyapunov
optimization framework and enjoy a competitive performance compared with the MPC method. In [52], a Lyapunov optimization-based
energy management strategy for the energy hub with an energy router
is proposed, and the proposed strategy can achieve an economic operation. In [53], an online energy management system based on Lyapunov
optimization is proposed to solve the stochastic optimal power flow
problem in microgrid; the performance is better than greedy policy and
close to optimal offline policy. In [54], a real-time bidirectional energy
control algorithm is developed for residential energy storage management system with integrated renewable generation; the proposed
method possesses a bounded performance guarantee compared with the
offline method. In [55], a Lyapunov optimization approach is proposed
to schedule the electric vehicle charging at fast charging stations. The
significant characteristic of Lyapunov optimization is that it can break
the time-coupling constraints of multistage stochastic problems, thus
the operator can solve one-time-slot problem to make real-time decisions without referring to future information. Besides, compared with
the dynamic programming method, it does not need offline training or
build optimal value function, showing its online adaptive and easy-toimplement advantage. Furthermore, the Lyapunov optimization-based
method can achieve better performance than the traditional greed
algorithm and bounded performance guarantee compared with optimal
offline results.
Given the above discussion, the P2P energy trading for networked
EHs with system uncertainty still meets the following two challenges.
(1) How to make real-time P2P trading decisions? In real-time operation, the P2P trading decision is made period by period in response to
the latest observation of renewable power, load, and prices. In practice,
the exact future uncertainty data is still not known because high-quality
renewable prediction in small regions is hard. However, the energy
storage unit operation depends on the overall renewable and load series
because of its limited capacity and time-coupling constraints. That is to
say, the decisions made in the current period will affect the decisions
in future periods, thus affecting the long-term operation economy.
Therefore, the uncertainty inherent in the system and the temporal
constraints of hybrid storage devices present significant obstacles to the
optimization of real-time P2P energy transactions within multiple EHs.
(2) How to fairly clear the P2P market in a distributed manner?
During the P2P energy transaction, peers should reach a consensus on
the energy pricing and quantities exchanged. However, the reluctance
of peers to disclose sensitive information poses a significant challenge
to the design of a decentralized decision-making framework. Besides,
because of the diversity of renewable and load, an energy hub might
be an energy supplier or demander in different scenarios; therefore, a
fair and efficient pricing scheme is necessary to incentive the dynamic
role transition and energy trading. Finally, it is crucial to develop a
mechanism that not only ensures fairness and efficiency in pricing but
also protects the private information.

To address the above issues, we propose an online decision scheme
for real-time P2P trading among networked energy hubs without renewable power predictions. The differences of this paper from the
existing relevant works are listed in Table 1. To begin with, we formulate a deterministic and centralized version of multiple energy hubs P2P
trading problem, whose goal is to minimize the long-term time-average
overall energy cost. Then centralized deterministic optimization model
is decomposed temporally and spatially. The contributions include:
(1) A temporal decomposition method based on Lyapunov optimization. The storage-level equation coupling decision in consecutive
periods is replaced by a queue stability condition, and the objective
function is modified as the weighted sum of cost and queue length,
called drift-plus-penalty. In this way, the multi-periods operation problem is decomposed into a single-period problem and can be solved
based on the current uncertainty realization, without referring to future
uncertainty information. The future impact of using energy storage is
taken into account by the cumulative queue length. We prove that
by properly selecting the parameter in the drift-plus-penalty function,
omitting storage level variables does not jeopardize storage capacity
constraints, and the optimality gap compared to the hindsight optimum
is bounded by a constant.
(2) A spatial decomposition method based on ADMM. To clear
the market and preserve privacy during P2P trading, ADMM is applied to the single-period problem with the drift-plus-penalty objective.
The subproblem of each energy hub is a quadratic program, which
is further decomposed into a strictly convex part and a linear programming part. The former has an analytical solution, and the latter
is solved via a customized vertex enumeration algorithm. Two parts
are coordinated via a golden section search scheme. In this way, the
proposed method can be implemented on simple hardware without
a sophisticated computation platform and optimization software. We
show that the proposed algorithm intrinsically determines the P2P
trading prices from the consensus-reaching process. The price discloses
the willingness of supply and consumption and promotes P2P trading.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. The hindsight operation problem of networked energy hubs is formulated in Section 2. The
temporal decomposition based on Lyapunov optimization is developed
in Section 3. The spatial decomposition based on ADMM and solution
routine of subproblems is presented in Section 4. The holistic flowchart
is plotted in Section 5. The case study is reported in Section 6. Finally,
Section 7 concludes the paper.

energy hub consists of some mainstream energy conversion and storage facilities, including combined-heat-power plant, electrical-powered
heat pump, as well as battery and thermal storage units; besides, the

battery, or be consumed by a heat pump which produces heat. Heat
can be directly used to supply the heat demand, or stored in a thermal
storage unit if necessary.
Since co-generation unit reuses waste heat, and excessive renewable
power can be converted to heat, the energy hub enjoys higher flexibility
compared to supplying power and heat separately. Furthermore, neighboring energy hubs can be connected via soft-open points, through
which the transmitted power between two energy hubs can be independently controlled. Although the renewable generation in each energy
hub may have a strong correlation, the demand profiles are different
and may exhibit some complementarity. The P2P energy trading can
further improve flexibility during real-time operation.
This paper aims to address two problems. First, as renewable generation is uncertain and deploying an advanced forecasting system
is expensive, how could each energy hub make real-time dispatch
decisions without prediction? Second, in P2P trading, how could each
energy hub price its energy while preserving privacy? The answers to
the above two problems are a natural consequence of the temporal and
spatial decomposition of the optimal operation problem, which will be
shown in the subsequent sections.

2.3.3. Challenges for solution
There are three difficulties that challenge the application of problem
(9) in practice:
(1) Constraints (3a)–(3b) couple decision variables in two successive
periods. As a result, the dispatch action in a period influences the
feasibility and cost in future periods. To identify the optimal solution,
we have to forecast 𝝃 𝒕 in all periods. However, it is difficult to get highquality renewable prediction in a small area, so problem (9) cannot be
established. If there are no time coupling constraints, then problem (9)
naturally decouples into 𝑇 single-period problems which can be solved
independently according to the observed value of 𝝃 𝒕 in each period.
This is a basic motivation of the proposed method.
(2) Problem (9) is cast in a centralized manner. To use this in
practice, some authority is responsible for collecting the data of all
energy hubs and solving problem (9), and then inform the optimal
action to each of them. However, system data, especially the consumption patterns, are private information that energy hubs are unwilling
to disclose. It is expected that the P2P market should be cleared in a
distributed way without exposing private data.
(3) To clear the P2P market in a distributed way, the energy hub
has to make individual decisions, and it is necessary to specify the
value of P2P trading price 𝜆𝑖𝑗 ,𝑡 , which does not appear in the centralized
problem (9). By now, it is not clear how to determine a fair price that
best incentivizes P2P trading.

6.7. Implications for energy market stakeholders
Given the above discussion in this paper, we provide the following
implications for grid operators, policymakers, energy consumers and
producers (prosumers).
(1) Grid operators: The implementation of P2P energy trading
methodologies, can augment the integration of distributed energy
resources (DERs), such as solar PV, wind energy, and storage unit.
Since renewable energy can be accommodated locally and the net load
demand of specific nodes is reduced, capacity expansion or update of
transmission lines, transformers, and other grid infrastructure can be
reduced or postponed. If the P2P traded energy is transferred through
the grid network, the grid operator needs to design a dispatch scheme
to adopt the novel business model to assure the security and reliability
of system operation.
(2) Policymakers: The cost reduction of peers and increased renewable utilization rate present policymakers with a compelling argument
to advocate for and to promulgate policies that foster the growth of P2P
energy trading markets. Based on our analysis, fair and efficient P2P
market structure is necessary. Besides, the market clearing mechanism
should also be incentive-compatible to attract more participants.
(3) Energy consumers and producers (Prosumers): The paradigm of
P2P energy trading brings a reduction in energy costs and an increase
in energy utilization efficiency for local peers. A peer should make good
dispatch decisions for its devices and determine the P2P traded energy
price and quantity. Our proposed online decision scheme provides a
prediction-free policy for sub-optimal device control, which can achieve
a nearly offline optimum result.
In summary, our research provides a corpus of valuable insights into
the prospective evolution of energy markets and offers a direction for
action to a diverse array of stakeholders.
