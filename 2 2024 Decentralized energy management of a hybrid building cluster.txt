Decentralized energy management of a hybrid building cluster via
peer-to-peer transactive energy trading
Chenhao Ying , Yunyang Zou , Yan Xu *
Center of Power Engineering (CPE), School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering, Nanyang Technological University, Singapore 639798, Singapore

H I G H L I G H T S

• A decentralized transactive energy management framework is designed for a real-world hybrid building cluster.
• The strategic interactions among the owners of distributed energy resources (DERs) and the consumers within the building cluster are captured by a multi‑leadermulti-follower Stackelberg game.
• A novel distributed algorithm is developed to protect the privacy and autonomy of participants in a large-scale building cluster.

With a rising number of buildings being equipped with private distributed energy resources (DERs) such as
rooftop PV panels and energy storage devices, an effective energy management method for a building cluster
becomes increasingly imperative. This paper proposes a novel decentralized transactive energy management
(TEM) method for a hybrid cluster of residential and commercial buildings, which enables peer-to-peer (P2P)
energy trading among the DER owners and consumers. The strategic interactions among the DER owners and
consumers are modeled as a multi‑leader-multi-follower (MLMF) Stackelberg game and formulated as a bi-level
model. The DER owners, the commercial and residential consumers are all autonomous entities, optimizing their
individual welfare functions and sharing necessary trading-related information, which are expressed as the
upper-level leaders’ models and the lower-level followers’ models, respectively. To preserve the privacy and
autonomy of each entity within the building cluster, a distributed algorithm incorporated with an efficient P2P
pricing mechanism is designed for the formulated MLMF Stackelberg game model. Simulation results demon­
strate the effectiveness of the proposed method on mitigating the reliance of the building cluster on the power
grid, motivating the DERs to actively participate in P2P trading, and reducing the consumers’ energy con­
sumption costs

1. Introduction
As an increasing level of distributed energy resources (DERs) are
deployed in both residential and commercial buildings, the uncertainty
and intermittence of DERs brings challenges to local power grid opera­
tion. To motivate the local consumption of DERs generation, the concept
of transactive energy (TE) emerges, which provides the providers and
consumers in a microgrid (MG) with a market platform to trade gener­
ated energy locally within their community hence promoting the local
energy supply-consumption balance [1–4].
In recent years, transactive energy management (TEM) has thus
received wide attention. The design of TEM can be generally classified

into two types: centralized TEM and decentralized TEM. To name a few,
[5] designs a centralized TE sharing approach for MG operators and
DER aggregators to minimize the total social cost. Ref. [6] provides a
centralized TE framework integrating a bilateral energy trading mech­
anism with the optimal power flow technique. Ref. [7] further considers
the limited capacity and controllability of TEM in multi-energy com­
plementary microgrids (MECMs), proposing a stochastic mechanismsupported strategy to coordinate interconnected MECMs. However,
their shortcoming is that the centralized TEM framework usually re­
quires a supervisor to collect the information of all participants, thus
bringing great concern of privacy protection.

the complicated interactions among multiple players [15]. Ref. [16]

However, when considering a fully decentralized TEM with multiple
energy producers rather than a single one, a SLMF game may be not
competent anymore.
To model the problem with multiple energy producers, [20] in­
troduces a multi-follower-multi‑leader (MLMF) Stackelberg game for
virtual microgrids (VMGs), which enables prosumers to compete to
satisfy their own goals. In [20], the producers, as leaders, evaluate their

trading based on the pricing information from the consumers. And the
consumers as followers consider the prices to determine their own bids
for these surplus energy. Ref. [21] generalizes the energy transaction
between the prosumers and end-users as a MLMF game, in which the
prosumers are leaders and end-users are followers, to increase the rev­
enues and reduce the peak load. However, most of these game-based
research works chose to reformulate the games into centralized opti­
mization problems for mathematical tractability. For instance, a SLMF
game is reformulated as a mathematical problem with equilibrium
constraints (MPEC) by using Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) optimality
conditions and a Big-M approach. Similarly, a MLMF game is rewritten
as an equilibrium problem with equilibrium constraints (EPEC).
Nevertheless, it should be mentioned that this type of centralized algo­
rithms could lead to a heavy computation burden and low solution ef­
ficiency due to the introduction of a great amount of integer variables.
Additionally, they also could lead to great concerns on privacy due to the
information collection required by these centralized algorithms.
To overcome these drawbacks, a distributed algorithm can be
considered. Refs. [17, 22] propose progress-hedging-based distributed
algorithms for multi-energy systems, decomposing the original large
problem into subproblems to be solved in parallel. Refs. [23–24] design
distributed algorithms to solve the centralized optimization problems
for cooperative and non-cooperative games. Ref. [25] proposes a
distributed algorithm for solving the energy consumption game among
followers in the SLMF game, allowing each follower to solve his own
optimization problem and publicly announce only necessary informa­
tion. For a MLMF game, [26] proposes a distributed algorithm for multienergy trading in an industrial park, which effectively reduces the in­
formation traffic, protects the users’ privacy, and reduces calculation
burdens. Ref. [27] designs a distributed algorithm for a P2P TE system
considering a TE adjustment mechanism to avoid the violation of P2P
energy transactions to network constraints. However, these research
works simulate only a few representative load agents or aggregators,
ignoring the diverse characteristics and preferences of end users. Such
assumption raises an important concern that the effectiveness of the
algorithm cannot be sufficiently guaranteed in a real-world scenario
involving large-scale building clusters.
There are few literatures on energy trading among buildings in a
real-world scale and some are formulated into bi-level models. However,
these works are more or less flawed. To be specific, [28] aims to save the
energy bill of the prosumers in commercial buildings by coordinating
their multi-energy equipment to participate in the P2P energy trading
among the commercial buildings. Nonetheless, the problem in this work
is modeled using a dual-pricing method and solved by a centralized al­
gorithm. Ref. [29] formulates the P2P energy trading between P2P
building prosumers and an independent system operator into a bi-level
model, which is solved with an ADMM-based distributed algorithm as in
[17,22]. But the model in [29] is modeled by dual pricing method,
rather than game theory. Similarly, [30] models double-auction P2P
energy trading among residential buildings equipped with PV-ESS sys­
tems, but solves the problem with a centralized algorithm. In addition,
user behaviors in these works are typically simplified, which however
should be further described more precisely by formulating respective
consumer utility functions and welfare models [31–33], especially for a
real-world large-scale building cluster to differentiate various end-users.

a cluster of residential and commercial buildings. The P2P energy
trading among the DER owners and consumers within the cluster is
modeled as a MLMF Stackelberg game, and then solved by a developed
iterative distributed algorithm. The differences between the existing

2. Proposed transactive energy management framework
Fig. 1 shows the proposed decentralized TEM framework for a hybrid
building cluster. The hybrid building cluster consists of residential and
commercial buildings with grid connected. Each building has been
equipped with rooftop solar PV panels and one battery energy storage
system (BESS), which are invested by respective DER owners. Within
each building, there exists numerous energy consumers such as apart­
ments, offices, stores, canteens, etc., who consume electricity to meet
their energy demands while increasing their utilities as much as
possible. The DER owner in each building is also profit-oriented. They
coordinate their private-invested DERs, and are allowed to feed surplus
power into the grid or sell it to the customers within the cluster for profit
maximization. In the building cluster, the consumers are classified into
residential and commercial consumers, who have different characteris­
tics and preferences on energy consumption, leading to different
responsive behaviors to market prices.

In the proposed P2P TEM, the strategic interactions among the DER
owners and consumers within the building cluster are captured by an
MLMF Stackelberg Game. Each DER owner acts as a leader, aiming to
maximize its owner welfare by coordinating the DERs and selling energy
at attractive prices. Each consumer acts as a follower responding to
leaders’ prices, seeking a trade-off energy consumption strategy be­
tween energy procurement cost and comfort level. In a Stackelberg
game, the DER owners take leading position as strategy makers, and the
consumers can only respond to the DER owners’ strategies as strategy
takers, which however will in turn influence the pricing and scheduling
strategies of DER owners.

model, consisting of the lower-level follower models and the upperlevel leader models. To facilitate the formulation of the Stackelberg

Typically, by replacing the lower-level models by their KKT opti­
mality conditions, the bi-level model (27)–(34) can be converted into an
EPEC and then solved by a centralized method [37]. The optimal so­
lution of EPEC responds to a Stackelberg equilibrium, where no player
will be willing to change their strategies unilaterally. However, since
KKT reformulation introduces in plenty of dual variables and comple­

Big-M method, Strong-Duality theory and other approaches are thus

numerous auxiliary binary 0–1 variables, which will pull down the so­
lution speed dramatically [1]. More importantly, regarding the P2P
TEM of a building cluster, a centralized method inevitably raises the
privacy and autonomy concerns of the followers considering it requires
the leaders to have a full knowledge of the followers’ information.
Hence, the limitations of a centralized method necessitate the devel­
opment of a distributed algorithm for the real-world implementation of
the proposed P2P TEM method.

5.3. Analysis of welfare and cost
To evaluate the influences of the proposed P2P TEM method on the
welfares and the electricity cost in different cases, Fig. 5(a) and (b)
shows the results of total owner welfare and total consumer welfare
respectively in the building cluster in time series, Table 7 shows the
comparison of owner welfare in detail, and Table 8 shows the compar­
ison of consumer welfare and electricity cost in detail.
As shown in Fig. 5(a), the differences in the negative owner welfares
in night time periods between Case I, II and Case III, IV result from
purchasing energy from grid to charge the BESS. Except for these pe­
riods, at most hours of a day, it is concluded that: 1) the owners with PV
and BESS can obtain more welfares than owners with only PV; 2) owners
with P2P TEM method obtain greater welfares than owners without the
proposed method. Note that the owner welfare at hour 15–16 and 18–19
in Case IV are much higher than Case IV just as mentioned previously.
The total owner welfare represents the same regularity as shown in
Table 7.
As shown in Fig. 5(b), since the residential loads dominate in night
time, there is no noticeable gap in consumer welfare. While at working
hours, with the help of the proposed P2P TEM method, the consumer
welfares in the building cluster in Case IV are apparently higher than
other cases, which represents higher consumer demands and the growth
on willingness of consuming energy from DERs.
According to Tables 7 and 8, when comparing Case I and II, the in­
crease in revenue from P2P transactions is greater than the decrease in
revenue from selling energy to grid in Case II, which leads to higher total
owner welfare. And the consumer utility increases as well, leading to a
higher consumer welfare in Case II. While in Case IV the higher owner
welfare compared to Case III results solely from increased revenue from
P2P transactions, the higher consumer welfare results from the utility
increase significantly exceeding the cost increase.
In conclusion, whether comparing Case I and II or comparing Case III
and IV, both the owner welfare and the consumer welfare increase after
applying the proposed method to the cluster. This indicates that the P2P
TEM method proposed in this paper benefits both DER owners and
consumers in the cluster.

6. Conclusion
In this paper, a P2P transactive energy management method is pro­
posed for a large-scale residential and commercial hybrid building
cluster with various consumers and DER owners. The trading in­
teractions among the DER owners and consumers in the cluster is
described by a Stackelberg game, and is formulated as a bi-level model,
where the consumers as followers optimize the consumer welfare in the
lower level, and the DER owners as leaders optimize their DER coordi­
nation and P2P pricing in the upper level. To protect privacy and au­
tonomy of each player while lowering the computation burdens, an
advanced distributed algorithm is developed in this paper. The results
demonstrate that: 1) the proposed P2P TEM method reduces the reliance
of the building cluster on the power grid during working periods; 2) the
method maximizes the potential of DERs (especially BESSs), enabling
P2P energy trading inside the cluster and contributing to the local
consumption of DERs; 3) each player has clear economic incentives to
participate in the designed decentralized transactive energy manage­
ment, as both the DER owners and consumers in the building cluster can
benefit from their participation; and 4) the proposed iterative distrib­
uted algorithm demonstrates its outstanding computational perfor­
mance, which greatly outperforms the existing centralized algorithms in
terms of solution time.
In this work, considering the redundant capacity of distribution
networks, the network constraints are omitted, which aims to focus on
addressing energy trading problems among market participants and
provide a timely reference for the decentralized transactive energy
management of a hybrid building cluster with DERs. In the future work,
efforts will be made to extend the proposed P2P transactive energy
management method to a set of building clusters, wherein physical
network constraints will be considered for guaranteeing networkconstraint-feasible energy trading actions.
