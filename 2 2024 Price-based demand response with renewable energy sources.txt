ABSTRACT
This article deals with the impact of demand response (DR) with peer-to-peer (P2P) energy
trading for a residential smart home consisting of smart appliances, an electric vehicle, a battery
energy storage system (BESS), and renewable energy-based distributed generation (DG) such as
solar PV and wind. The proposed work deals with two stages, such as DR implementation and
P2P energy trading. The first stage of implementation deals with the optimal scheduling of smart
appliances in a residential home using a Binary Dragonfly Algorithm (BDA). Each appliance in
the smart home is scheduled based on the slab tariff, Time of Day (ToD), and Real-Time Pricing
(RTP) with DR to identify the most economical tariff structure for the residential home. Five
different cases are examined to identify the best smart home for effectively utilizing DGs by
reducing grid dependency and electricity costs.The RTP tariff is proved to be more beneficial
than the slab tariff and ToD tariff because the electricity cost of the smart home is very minimal
for all the cases with RTP, especially in case 5 cost is reduced by |12.0613 than slab tariff and
|8.414 than ToD. Hence, the RTP tariff is chosen as the optimal tariff structure for the next stage
of the proposed work.The second stage of implementation deals with the P2P trading between
the smart homes with a proposed enhanced bidding strategy to reduce the grid dependency
and electricity cost of the individual smart homes. The proposed enhanced bidding strategy
involves a double auction mechanism to determine the trading decision and trading cost for the
benefit of both consumers and prosumers. The electricity cost reduction achieved by consumer
1 and consumer 2 from the enhanced smart bidding strategy are |92.7615 and |15.1525,
respectively. The results also proved that prosumer 1 and prosumer 2 obtained a profit of
|78.2170 and |28.6970, respectively. The EV uncertainty analysis is also examined in smart
homes to validate the effectiveness of the proposed bidding strategy. The simulation results
proved that the proposed DR-based P2P trading with an enhanced bidding strategy reduces
grid dependency and electricity costs, including EV uncertainty conditions.

The benefits of DR have been enhanced by effectively using solar photovoltaic (PV) in [10–13]. In [10], Time of Use (ToU)
tariff-based DR has been implemented with PV and battery energy storage systems (BESS) to minimize electricity costs and peak
demand. Similarly, the DR implementation with PV and battery has been implemented for smart home, but the limited number of
appliances have been considered, and the thermal loads have not been considered, which affects the user comfort [11]. However,
temperature-based modeling of thermal loads has been considered for DR implementation in [12]. In [13], the DR implementation
with variable PV penetration was analyzed, and the developed rule-based model was proved to minimize the electricity cost for
both high and low penetrations of PV power. However, the cost minimization is comparatively higher for high PV penetration than
for low PV penetration [13]. The RTP-based DR has been implemented in the smart home with DG and battery, along with the
consideration of thermal load modeling to reduce electricity costs and improve user comfort [14]. In [15], solar panels have been
included in the Residential Energy Hubs (REHs) to balance costs and energy efficiency. Additionally, the literature has focused on
the integration of heat storage units in REHs to reduce solar energy losses without significantly increasing costs. However, the major
limitation of the literature discussed above is the lack of better utilization of DG power and battery power for all the time slots. The
electricity cost could be reduced further by effectively using the DGs and battery power by implementing P2P energy trading.
P2P energy trading allows prosumers to trade the excess power available from their DGs after self-consumption with the
neighboring consumers [16]. P2P energy trading provides many benefits to consumers and prosumers, such as encouraging the
utilization of renewable-based DGs and improving economic welfare through the better utilization of DGs. Additionally, the utility
grid also gets benefits, such as reduced peak demand, reduced cost of investment on peak power generation, and improved grid
reliability [16]. In [17], prosumers with excess power due to high penetration of PV have traded the power to the consumers
through a bidding strategy, where the aggregator decides the trading based on the bid and quoted values from the consumers and
prosumers. The trading cost has been determined as the mean value of the bid and quote values. On the other hand, the prosumers
were clustered using game theory based on the load profile and power availability, where trading was done between only prosumers,
but the consumers were not considered in the system [18]. Similarly, prosumer-centric P2P trading has been suggested in [19] to
improve the welfare of the prosumers by considering PV and Electric Vehicle (EV). In [19], the trading was done based on a double
auction mechanism, while the trading tariff was determined at a mid-market rate. On the other hand, multi-agent Peer-to-Peer dairy
farm energy simulator-extended (MAPDES) has been developed in [20] with auction-based energy trading to significantly reduce
electricity costs, decrease peak demand, and increase energy sales for dairy farms participating in peer-to-peer markets. In [20], the
DR implementation is not considered, which is a major limitation.
The P2P trading framework, which considers the trading between prosumers and consumers, has been discussed in [21–24].
The trading tariff was determined based on the levelized cost of electricity, whereas the trading decision was made by selecting the
best combination from all the possible combinations of trading and trading tariffs between prosumers and consumers. At the same
time, the same process becomes complex when the system is larger [21]. On the other hand, retailers have been considered along
with the consumers and prosumers in [22]. The trading tariff has been calculated based on the individual prosumer’s cost equation
based on the excess energy availability. Similarly, the cost function-based tariff determination based on the current and past values
of energy and trading tariffs to improve the welfare of the prosumers and consumers has been discussed in [23]. In [24], the double
auction mechanism based on P2P trading between prosumers and consumers has been developed. The trading decision was made
by following the supply and demand equilibrium, where the aggregator matches the bid and quote values. The literature discussed
above has focused on improving the welfare of prosumers and consumers, but the benefits could be further improved by considering
DR and P2P trading. There is a need for DR with P2P energy trading to better utilize DGs and reap economic benefits [25].
Mixed integer programming has been suggested for the RTP-based electricity market with renewable energy sources, storage
systems, and electric vehicles for economic and environmental benefits [26]. It has been proved that the DR with RTP has
significantly increased the retailer profit and energy supply security value as compared with fixed tariff pricing. But, the literature
has not considered consumer behavior and user comfort. In [27], the RTP-based DR and P2P energy trading for the distribution
network for the peak demand reduction is discussed. The trading tariff is considered the same as the feed-in tariff. In [27], the
aggregator made the trading decision irrespective of the profit of the prosumer. The DR-based P2P trading is developed in [28] to
improve the revenue of the prosumers and reduce the grid dependency. Self-sufficiency and self-consumption ratio are considered
to prioritize the self-consumption of power from DGs over the P2P trading. But the trading framework considered prosumers alone
neglecting the consumers. Likewise, the demand side load management and P2P trading between the prosumers is developed for the
reduction in grid dependency and congestion in the grid in [29]. The market modeling is not done in [29]. The trading decision and
tariff are decided so that the congestion is reduced. Hence, it is beneficial only to the grid and the prosumers are less benefitted. The
DR-based P2P energy trading is implemented in a microgrid with prosumers and consumers for the electricity cost reduction and
peak demand reduction [30]. The trading tariff is considered as fixed based on the incentive for the microgrid node. The literature
discussed above considered the DR implementation by scheduling power demand instead of scheduling individual appliances. On
the other hand, a two-stage trading framework is suggested in [31], where the first stage is appliance scheduling and the second
stage is P2P trading. However, only three appliances are considered, and user comfort is not considered.

1.1. Contributions of the proposed work
From the detailed literature survey, the research gap is identified as the lack of proper scheduling of power demand by considering
all categories of appliances, proper utilization of DG power to minimize the grid dependency, and optimal trading models for
consumers. This proposed research work addresses these limitations by incorporating demand response (DR) and peer-to-peer (P2P)
energy trading to minimize electricity cost and grid dependency using the Binary Dragonfly Algorithm (BDA) and an enhanced
bidding strategy. The DR implementation focuses on minimizing electricity costs, while P2P energy trading aims to reduce grid
dependency. Additionally, the DR implementation optimizes scheduling and enhances the utilization of DG power, while P2P energy
trading, using an enhanced bidding strategy, aims to reduce grid dependency..
The contributions of the proposed work are given below:
1. The proposed work consists of two stages: The DR implementation for optimal scheduling of the appliances in prosumers
and consumers as the first stage. Three different tariff structures, slab tariff, ToD, and RTP, are compared to find the most
economical tariff structure.
2. The smart appliances in the smart homes are scheduled using the BDA based on the RTP, status of DGs, battery & EV, and
appliance operating constraints without affecting the user comfort. Smart appliances can be categorized as controllable and
uncontrollable loads with electric/thermal loads.
3. The excess power availability from the prosumers after their consumption can be traded to the neighboring consumer through
P2P trading with the most economical tariff structure, which is the second stage of the proposed work.
4. The trading decision is determined using the enhanced smart bidding strategy with the proposed double auction mechanism
by considering the bidding and quote values of consumers and prosumers. The supply–demand ratio (SDR) and RTP tariff
estimate the bidding and quote values.
5. The trading tariff is determined based on the SDR (calculated based on the trading decision) and RTP tariff such that the
proposed trading tariff is always less than the grid tariff at all time slots.
The remaining sections of the article are organized as follows: Section 2 describes the proposed system for DR and bidding-based
P2P trading with DR implementation, Section 3 explains the mathematical modeling of DR, Peer-to-peer trading using enhanced
bidding strategy, and BDA for DR implementation, Section 4 discusses the simulation results obtained from the proposed system
with EV uncertainty analysis and Section 5 ends with the conclusion.

2.1. Demand response
In the first stage of implementation, only one smart home is considered from the proposed residential microgrid which is
portrayed in Fig. 1. Five different cases are formulated for the smart home based on the availability of DGs, battery power and
EV battery power to identify the optimal electricity reduction of the smart home and minimum grid dependency. Case 1 considers
the smart home without DGs and EV battery, case 2 considers the smart home with PV and EV battery, case 3 considers the smart
home with PV, Battery and EV battery, case 4 considers the smart home with PV, wind, and EV battery and case 5 considers the
smart home with PV, wind, battery, and EV battery. The scheduling of appliances in the smart home is carried out by considering the
user-preferred appliance operation time with DR implementation. The time window and duration of each appliance are framed based
on the usage pattern of each appliance. The operating time slot of the appliances without affecting the user comfort is determined
using BDA algorithm by considering the grid tariff, available power form solar photovoltaic (PV), wind power generation, battery,
and EV battery in the proposed smart home to minimize grid dependency and electricity costs. The EV battery acts as storage device
during its idle period. Also, three different tariff structures are considered to decide the most economical tariff for the smart home.
Due to the availability of DGs, excess power may be available in some time slots after their self-consumption which is injected into
the grid with feed-in-tariff. In order to improve the profit for the smart home, the available excess power which is traded to the
neighboring smart homes using P2P energy trading, which is the second stage of the proposed work.
2.2. Peer-to-peer trading
In the second stage of implementation, a proposed residential microgrid consisting of four smart homes is considered for P2P
trading. Fig. 2 illustrates the residential microgrid considered in the proposed work. Smart home 1 and smart home 2 are considered
as the prosumer1 and prosumer 2 whereas smart home 3 and smart home 4 are considered as consumer 1 and consumer 2. Prosumer
1 is considered the eighteen different appliances along with the different power sources like case 5. Prosumer 2 is considered the
fifteen different appliances along with the different power sources like case 2. On the other hand, consumer 1 is similar to case 1,
where EV battery is alone considered with the absences of DGs. Consumer 2 is considered only 15 different smart appliances alone.
The power demand of the prosumers & consumers is determined with DR implementation on each smart home appliance with the
most economical tariff structure. The DGs power from the prosumers may be excess after their self-consumption, which can be traded
to the neighboring consumers based on the double-auction mechanism. The double-auction mechanism allows the prosumers and
consumers to submit their quote and bid values. The quote and bid values are calculated based on the supply–demand ratio (SDR)
of individual homes and grid tariff. The trading decision is finalized based on the calculated bid and quote values. For instance, the
highest bid value of the consumer and least quote value of the prosumer are prioritized for the trading process. The next higher
values are considered for the subsequent trading process. After the trading decision is made, the trading tariff for prosumers is
calculated based on the SDR, grid tariff and feed-in-tariff.

3.2. Peer-to-peer trading using enhanced bidding strategy
In the proposed work, the P2P energy trading is done by enhanced bidding strategy. The energy management system of the
individual smart home decides the power requirement, or availability of excess power after their self-consumption which can be
communicated to the neighboring smart homes. In case of power requirement of the smart home, it receives the power from the
grid or trading based on the grid tariff or trading tariff. In case of excess power available, it provides the quote value to decide the
trading and its trading tariff. The trading process follows the enhanced bidding strategy that involves double-auction mechanism
to decide the trading decision and trading tariff. The proposed enhanced bidding strategy involves the calculation of SDR in two
different phases. The first SDR calculation decides the trading decision based on the bid and quote values by considering the total
power availability from the prosumers for trading and power demand requirement on the consumers. The second SDR decides the

4.1. Demand response
In this section, the simulation results of single smart home with DR implementation for five different cases are discussed along
with three different tariff structures such as slab tariff, ToD tariff and RTP tariff. The five different cases are considered based on
the available resources and storage devices. The optimal scheduling of different appliances using BDF algorithm for a 24- hour
time slots by considering the user comfort, DG power, battery and EV battery to reduce the cost of electricity is done in MATLAB
2022. The maintenance cost for PV, wind and battery is considered as 1 |/kwh, whereas the feed in tariff for the power injected
into the grid is considered as 2 |/kwh. The power rating, operation time window and its duration of the different appliances is
tabulated in Table 2. The specifications of the battery and EV battery considered in this work are listed in Table 3. The different
cases with different tariff structures are examined in the proposed smart home and results are obtained. The RTP for hourly electricity
tariff is obtained from [46], which is listed in Table 4. The smart home demand is continuously varying for 24-hours due to their
power consumption by the appliances, which depends on the operating time duration and time window. In this work, the primary
importance is given to fulfill the smart home demand due to power consumed by the appliances. When the power is only available
from DG in the smart home, it is utilized for the smart home demand and in case the excess power available after fulfilling the smart
home demand, which shall be injected into the grid. On the other hand, the battery is considered along with DG, the charging and
discharging of the battery is determined on the available SoC, power demand of the smart home and availability of DG power. If
the SoC of the battery is less than a minimum limit, the battery is charged. When the SoC of battery is greater than a maximum
limit, the battery is discharged. At the same time, the battery SoC level is within the maximum and minimum limits, the battery
charging /discharging is determined based on the power demand of the smart home and electricity tariff at the time slot.

4.2. P2P energy trading with prosumer to consumer power transfer
Recently, P2P energy trading has become very popular in reducing grid dependency. Therefore, peer-to-peer energy trading with
RTP tariff is done between four smart homes to examine the reduction of grid dependency and electricity cost of the individual smart
home. The two smart homes are prosumers, where the first prosumer consists of 18 different smart appliances, solar PV, wind, BESS,
EV battery, and the second prosumer consists of only 15 different smart appliances, solar PV and EV battery. The difference between
smart homes is considered to showcase real-time scenarios. On the other hand, the remaining two smart homes are consumers
where the first consumer consists of only 18 different smart appliances and EV battery, and the second consumer consists of only
15 different smart appliances. The power demand of the prosumers and consumers is determined by the DR based on the economic
tariff structure. After self-consumption, the excess power available to the prosumers is traded to the neighboring prosumers and
consumers based on a double-auction mechanism. Fig. 10 illustrates the power demand of the prosumers and consumers with
excess power availability of prosumers in the residential microgrid. The negative values of first and second prosumers show the
excess power availability after self-consumption of power demand. The positive values of first and second prosumers show the grid
power consumption. Consumers only have power demand, so they do not obtain negative values due to the non-availability of DGs.
Excessive power from prosumers is traded with consumers using an enhanced bidding strategy.

4.2.1. P2P trading with prosumer to consumer power transfer under EV uncertainty
This section analyzes the impact of uncertain EV behavior in P2P trading to examine the effectiveness of the enhanced bidding
strategy. This work executes the EV uncertainty by increasing the EV driving range by 100 km. Due to the increment of driving
range, the EV operation in driving mode is extended by 2 h, i.e., arrival time is shifted from 19:00 to 21:00. Due to the increase in
EV discharge power due to extended driving range, the SoC level of the EV battery is reduced. Hence, the EV needs to be charged
immediately after returning from the trip to maintain the minimum required SoC level of the EV battery. The extended driving
range of the battery EV is considered for the first consumer and first prosumer. The EV uncertainty in the first consumer increases
power demand due to EV charging. On the other hand, EV uncertainty in the first prosumer reduces the excess power availability
for P2P trading due to the increased power demand of the first prosumer.
Fig. 13 portrays the P2P power transfer, the trading cost for the P2P transfer, grid power consumption, and the net electricity
of the prosumers and consumers after P2P trading under EV uncertainty. Figs. 13(a) and 13(b) show that the P2P trading from the
first prosumer to the consumers decreases to zero at 23:00 compared to normal conditions. Also, the power traded is reduced at
21:00 & 24:00 due to the EV battery charging of the first prosumer due to EV uncertainty compared to the normal condition. From
Fig. 13(c), it is clearly visible that the grid power consumption of the first consumer may increase during the time slot of 22:00
to 24:00 due to the EV charging and lack of excess power for P2P trading from the first prosumer compared to normal conditions.
Also, the first prosumer consumes power from the grid at 23:00 due to the demand for EV charging. Figure 17(d) illustrates the
net electricity cost of the prosumers and consumers after P2P trading under EV uncertainty. Fig. 13(d) shows the electricity cost of
the consumers, where it achieves a minimum from the time slot of 09:00 to 16:00 due to the P2P trading. Also, the first prosumer
achieves profit by trading the power to the first consumer at 22:00 due to battery discharging despite EV uncertainty.

5. Conclusion
In this article, the impact of DR implementation and P2P energy trading in residential smart homes to reduce electricity costs and
grid dependency has been examined. The proposed enhanced bidding strategy has been implemented to decide the trading decision
and trading cost for P2P energy trading. The proposed work has been done in two different stages. The first stage of the proposed
work represents the implementation of demand response with three different tariffs to schedule the smart appliances optimally in
the residential smart home using BDA optimization. Based on the simulation results of five cases in a smart home, the RTP tariff
has been identified as the most optimal tariff structure among the slab, ToD, and RTP tariffs in all the five cases. The electricity
cost of cases 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5 are reduced as compared with a slab tariff are 13.35%, 41.11%, 13.079%, 14.99% and 16.21%,
respectively. Similarly, the electricity cost reduction with the RTP tariff as compared with ToD is 5.156%, 13.079%, 14.99%, and
16.2074%, respectively. Hence, the electricity cost of the smart home has been reduced with the optimal scheduling of residential
appliances, PV, wind, BESS, and EV battery. The excess power available after the self-consumption has been injected into the grid
with a feed-in tariff. The second stage of the proposed work represents the implementation of P2P energy trading to reduce the grid
dependency further for smart homes and improve the profit for the smart home by reducing the electricity costs. Therefore, the
excess power available from the prosumers has been traded to neighboring consumers or prosumers with the proposed enhanced
bidding strategy instead of injected into the grid based on the power requirement. The electricity cost of the smart home is reduced
