The integration of prosumers as sustainable and decentralized units has become a fundamental aspect of smart
energy systems. It is therefore crucial to implement a market-oriented scheduling framework that guarantees the
autonomous operation of prosumers while safeguarding their privacy, enhancing operational autonomy, and
promoting competitiveness in electricity markets. In this regard, this paper puts forth a prosumer-centered
approach to smart prosumer operations management, with the objective of enabling their participation in
decentralized peer-to-peer (P2P) energy markets. To address this challenge, a multi-agent twin delayed deep
deterministic policy gradient (MATD3) algorithm with a decentralized training and execution (DTDE) strategy is
implemented, enabling prosumers to independently manage their demand, energy storage systems (ESS), and
bidding/asking prices. This approach determines the demand of prosumers and the quantities of energy to be
charged/discharged by their ESS in a dynamic manner, with the objective of making robust decisions regarding
uncertainties arising from consumption and generation. Furthermore, a novel pricing mechanism comprising the
mid-market rate (MMR) and MATD3 is proposed for decentralized P2P transactions within an adaptive
continuous double auction (ACDA) market. Finally, a case study of 20 residential prosumers is presented,
collectively engaged in P2P transactions to illustrate the efficacy of the proposed framework by achieving 9.8%
and 13.2% scheduling errors, 5.2% P2P participation, and 10% revenue increment.

1.1. Background and motivation
The transition toward more sustainable and decentralized energy
systems has gained momentum in recent years [1]. Furthermore, the
growing adoption of distributed energy resources (DER) at the house­
hold level, including rooftop photovoltaic (PV) panels and ESSs, has
fundamentally transformed the nature of the household sector, giving
rise to the concept of the prosumer [2]. In this context, prosumers
possess the potential to serve as a crucial driving force in the realization
of smart energy communities and the advancement of smart grid ap­
plications [3].
Conversely, the imperative for intelligent facility management rep­
resents a further significant challenge. In particular, the independent
and simultaneous operation of optimal demand/supply scheduling and
optimal management of facilities represents sophisticated issues that
require appropriate approaches [4]. Traditional system-centric ap­
proaches may not be well-suited for managing such complex in­
teractions and processes involved in prosumer management issues.
Consequently, significant attention has been devoted to the field of
prosumer operation studies by both academic and industrial sectors,
given the potential benefits these entities offer for energy communities
and smart grids. One of the principal applications of the smart grid that
can fully leverage the capabilities of prosumers is the establishment of
P2P energy trading markets. In addition to direct energy exchanges, P2P
markets provide an opportunity for prosumers to capitalize on their
generation, consumption, and storage capabilities, thereby enabling
their participation in decentralized energy management [5]. By
participating in P2P markets, prosumers can not only manage their
energy utilization and costs but also contribute to the overall flexibility
and efficiency of the smart grid [6,7].
Nevertheless, the successful implementation of P2P energy trading
markets is contingent upon the capacity to effectively manage the
intricate interactions and decision-making processes inherent to pro­
sumer communities. In this context, the application of advanced artifi­
cial intelligence techniques, such as deep reinforcement learning (DRL),
has emerged as a promising solution to address the intricate interactions
and decision-making processes of smart and autonomous prosumers [8].
The DRL algorithm relies on the trial-and-error mechanism in its in­
teractions with the environment to predict the agent's actions [9]. In this
regard, prosumers are regarded as independent agents in multi-agent
DRL (MADRL) programming, capable of learning optimal policies
through interactions with their environment. By employing the MADRL,
prosumers can adapt their energy management strategies in a dynamic
manner. This encompasses demand load scheduling, ESS operational
planning, and the determination of bidding and asking prices in P2P
markets. The strategies are informed by local observations and an un­
derstanding of market evolution conditions.

2.2.1. Trading mechanism
To perform P2P exchanges within the context of the proposed
decentralized auction-based market, this study proposes the use of the
ACDA mechanism.
To enhance the adaptivity among buyers and sellers, the proposed
ACDA mechanism is formed by combining two standard clearing algo­
rithms: the matching algorithm and the clearing price algorithm. The
matching algorithm is employed to align bids and asks in accordance
with the price priority factor. In the context of this study, higher bid
prices or lower ask prices are accorded priority over lower bid prices or
higher ask prices. In the event of identical prices, the number of pro­
sumers is considered a priority. This algorithm guarantees that the
trading price reflects the most competitive bids and asks in the market.
Subsequently, the clearing price algorithm is employed to calculate the
P2P trading price based on the intersection of bid and ask prices.
This hybrid algorithm can improve efficiency and fairness in iden­
tifying potential matches, promote flexibility in determining the trans­
action price and quantity, and represent a realistic essence of market
transaction dynamics.

which defines the probability of transitioning from state s to state sʹ after

c) Parameter sharing: In the parameter sharing paradigm, all agents
have the capacity to leverage the experiences of other agents during
training by sharing a subset of their neural network parameters,
thereby enhancing the learning performance. Additionally, each
agent retains its own copy of the policy/value network, with select
layers being shared across agents. This shared component is
responsible for learning a generalized representation based on the
collective experience of all agents. The primary benefit of parameter

violate the privacy of agents. In light of these considerations, this
strategy can be advantageous in multi-agent energy trading systems
where coordination between agents is necessary or where there is a
need for coordinated operation, similar to the approach taken in the
CTDE approach.
d) Decentralized training with decentralized execution (DTDE): In the
DTDE approach, both the training and execution phases are con­
ducted by each agent in an independent manner. This implies that
each agent monitors its own state and executes actions based on its
unique experience (learned policy), without exchanging information
with other agents. The DTDE approach is advantageous for systems
with fully independent operations where coordination or commu­
nication between agents is not a consideration and the primary
objective is to preserve privacy. Furthermore, this paradigm facili­
tates heterogeneous policy learning, which is advantageous in cases
where agents have disparate reward structures or seek to satisfy
disparate personal preferences. In light of these considerations, this
approach represents the optimal strategy for fully decentralized
multi-agent energy transaction systems, wherein each agent operates
independently, assuming responsibility for its own training and
execution based on individual datasets.

process is governed by two neural networks: the actor and the critic. The
actor network takes the current state as input and extracts a single
continuous action value, representing the agent's action in the envi­
ronment. In contrast, the critic network is responsible for estimating the
value function (Q-value) for a given state-action pair. This involves
evaluating the quality of the chosen action by estimating the expected
future reward. In addition, TD3 employs two separate critic networks,
referred to as the “Twin Critics”. The minimum of these two critic out­
puts is then employed as the target for updating the other critic network,
thereby ensuring a more conservative and realistic value estimation and
mitigating the overestimation bias inherent in traditional Q-learning
methods. Moreover, TD3 incorporates delayed policy updates, whereby
the critic networks are updated more frequently than the actor network.
TD3 also incorporates target network updates, whereby separate target
networks are utilized for computing the target values and updated
slowly by a soft update rule. These mechanisms facilitate enhanced
stability and convergence.
During the training phase, the agents interact with the environment,
collect the relevant transitions (i.e., state, action, reward, next state, and
done), and store them in the replay buffer. Subsequently, random min­
ibatches of transitions are sampled from the replay buffer for the pur­
pose of updating the actor and critic networks. The actor network is
updated by maximizing the output of the critic network, taking into
account the actions generated by the actor network. In turn, the critic
networks are updated by sampled transitions and target values
computed from the target actor and critic networks. Subsequently, a soft
update of the target actor and critic networks is performed using the
main actor and critic networks. The replay buffer facilitates experience
replay, a technique that enhances data efficiency and de-correlates the
samples, thereby promoting more stable learning.
In the developed TD3, the exploration process is modified through
the introduction of Gaussian noise into the action space. The noise level
is initially set to a high value and subsequently decays over time, thereby
promoting exploration during the initial stages of the learning process
and exploitation during the subsequent stages.
2.3.2. Strategies of MADRL algorithm
In addition to the structural algorithm of MATD3, selecting an
appropriate strategy for multi-agent system learning is a crucial aspect
that warrants particular attention. Regarding this matter, there are four
strategies for MADRL algorithms, classified according to their training
and execution [22], which are as follows:
a) Centralized training and centralized execution (CTCE): This paradigm
involves both training and execution in a centralized manner. In
CTCE, the aim is to train a joint model containing all agents' obser­
vations (states) and actions with a centralized trainer. This central­
ized trainer learns a centralized policy (or value function) and takes
actions for all agents simultaneously. The CTCE paradigm is appli­
cable when agents are capable of communicating and sharing in­
formation during the execution phase, and the issue of agent privacy
is not a concern. In particular, this procedure requires significant
scalability and dimensionality due to the exponential growth in the
observation and action spaces with the increase in agent numbers.
Given these considerations, this strategy is not well-suited for use in
real-world and decentralized multi-agent systems where information
security is of paramount importance and agents require rapid
responses.
b) Centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE): The CTDE
approach represents an additional strategy for the MADRL frame­
work, wherein agents are trained in a centralized manner but execute
their policies in a decentralized way. During the training phase,
agents have access to the full range of information, including the

In alignment with the aforementioned strategies, this paper is
intended to utilize the DTDE approach as a convenient decentralized and
independent paradigm for the proposed system. In this approach, pri­
vacy is inherently preserved through the use of training and execution in
decentralized manners. Under DTDE, each prosumer agent operates
independently by processing its own data and learning its optimal policy
in a local manner without sharing raw data with other agents. This local
processing and learning ensure that sensitive information does not leave
the individual prosumer's environment, thus eliminating the risks
associated with centralized data aggregation, including data breaches
and unauthorized access.

In the proposed framework, the employed state transition method is
based on the random selection process to extract transition functions for
demand load and ESS charge/discharge from historical data. The
random selection of the subsequent state ensures that the learning
process is not deterministic but stochastic, and is not contingent on
precise probability distributions for disparate uncertainties [8]. In other
words, the “random selection process” means that at each training
episode, the state variables (e.g., demand load, PV generation) are
drawn from the historical dataset using random sampling with
replacement. Each historical record (demand, PV generation, and asso­
ciated variables at a specific timestamp) is sampled as a complete unit,
ensuring that the natural correlation between demand and PV is fully
preserved. Sampling is not restricted to any specific hour range, season,
or type of day; instead, every historical observation has an equal prob­
ability of being selected at every time step. This method preserves the
empirical distribution of the real data while avoiding deterministic
repetition, thereby ensuring variability and robustness in the agent's
learning process. This process enables agents to learn robust policies in
stochastic scenarios and account for uncertainties in consumption and
generation patterns.

This section is intended to provide a detailed account of the P2P
market implementation, based on the proposed structure outlined in
Section 2.2. As previously stated, this market is a decentralized P2P
market that employs an innovative ACDA mechanism. In order to
facilitate the execution of P2P transactions, the market maker initially
collates the data pertaining to the participation of all prosumers. Sub­
sequently, the data is sorted into bids and asks based on the matching
algorithm and the bid/ask price priority mechanism, whereby the
highest price bids are matched with the lowest price asks. Afterward,
P2P exchanges are conducted between any buyer and seller pair based
on the sorted list. The P2P exchange price is then calculated through the
clearing algorithm by the intersection of bid and ask prices. In the event
that any buyer or seller remains unsatisfied after P2P transactions, a
centralized retail market is considered to clear them with retail market
prices. The aforementioned procedure of market transactions is pre­
sented in Algorithm 4 for further clarification.

For more clarity, it is important to emphasize that the ACDA mech­
anism allows each buyer and seller to participate in multiple bilateral
transactions within the same time period. This behavior results from
ACDA's iterative allocation rule: once a buyer matches with the lowestprice available seller, the buyer's remaining unmet demand (if any) al­
lows further matches with the next seller in the merit order. A similar
process applies to sellers with remaining available energy.
For instance, at Time 8, buyer P1 initially matches with seller P19
(due to its lower ask price than P6) until either P1's demand or P19's
available surplus is fully allocated. If the buyer still has unmet demand
after the first match, the mechanism continues to match it with the next

seller, resulting in multiple transactions within the same time interval.
Subsequently, the same process happens for the seller with surplus
generation. In this case, P1 still has unmet demand after the first match.
So, ACDA proceeds to match P1 with the next available seller (i.e., P6),
thereby producing multiple bilateral trades within the same hour.
Moreover, the P2P clearing price for each match is determined accord­
ing to the ACDA pricing rule, based on the intersection of the buyer's bid
price and the seller's ask price (expressed in $/kWh).
Figs. 8 and 9 illustrate the cumulative P2P exchange costs and net
clearing costs of each prosumer over the course of the test day, respec­
tively. Accordingly, the most active participants in the ACDA market as
sellers are identified as prosumers 2, 8, and 15. Conversely, on the test
day, prosumers 1, 4, 5, and 10 were the largest purchasers in the ACDA.
Moreover, the net purchasing quantities are the greatest for prosumers 3
and 10, while the net sold quantities are the greatest for prosumers 9, 13,
and 16. In light of the aforementioned findings, it can be concluded that
the community of prosumers in question requires a total of 207.8 kW of
electricity on the test day. A total of 10.75 kW was fulfilled by P2P
transactions, representing 5.2% of the daily electricity requirements.
While this share may appear modest, it is well justified by the archi­
tecture of the modeled system. The primary reasons behind this partic­
ipation level are aligned with two factors:
First, all of the prosumers are households equipped with identical
ESS capacity and PV panels sized in proportion to their consumption.
This design results in similar generation and consumption patterns
across the community. Such homogeneity restricts the occurrence of
complementary supply/demand pairs, thereby narrowing the temporal
overlap for effective P2P transactions. Second, the MATD3 learning
process drives agents to adopt rational, risk-sensitive behaviors, further
disincentivizing speculative or marginal trades. These conditions natu­
rally constrain the volume of bilateral energy exchanges.
It is important to emphasize that this result does not indicate a
limitation of the proposed model but rather reflects the underlying
symmetry in system design. It means that implementing more diverse
DER configurations (e.g., CHP units or heterogeneous ESS capacities)
among prosumers, or even considering the combination of prosumers/
consumers that overlaps the surplus generation with requirements,
could broaden the scope for asymmetric energy behavior and thus
enable a greater share of P2P trading. For instance, prosumers with
larger PV arrays (or containing additional generation units like CHP)
would benefit from surplus generation at times when others still face
deficits, while prosumers with larger ESS capacities have this capability
to shift more energy availability across periods in ways that differ from
smaller-storage peers.

In the context of energy markets, market volatility poses a substan­
tial challenge, manifesting as sudden fluctuations in energy prices or
prosumers' demand/supply. These fluctuations can give rise to unstable
bid/ask prices and distort market outcomes. Conventional centralized
pricing methodologies, predicated on retailer-based systems, or static
pricing techniques, such as MMR-based methods, are particularly sus­
ceptible to such price fluctuations. This often gives rise to pricing dis­
tortions and a concomitant diminution in economic efficiency.
The proposed MATD3-based framework addresses this drawback

through its adaptive, decentralized design. Specifically, the dynamic
learning capabilities of MATD3 allow each prosumer agent to continu­
ously update its pricing decisions in real-time, based on local observa­
tions and evolving market conditions. This continuous adjustment
process enables the system to dampen the impact of abrupt market
changes and maintain competitive pricing even under volatile condi­
tions. By doing so, the proposed framework not only mitigates the
adverse effects of volatility but also preserves the advantages of costsaving, profit optimization, and fair pricing.
5. Conclusion
This study has proposed a novel MADRL framework for the man­
agement of smart prosumers in decentralized P2P energy markets. The
primary contributions of this study are as follows: (1) The imple­
mentation of the MATD3 algorithm with a DTDE strategy, which allows
for fully independent prosumer operation. (2) The dynamic determina­
tion of demand load and energy storage operation. The proposed
framework addresses the challenges of independent operation, privacy
