1. Introduction

1.1. Motivation

To increase the penetration of clean energy, many buildings
ave installed their own photovoltaic (PV) modules and energy
torage systems (ESS). PV modules bring clean energy to building
sers, and household ESS can optimize electricity consumption
ehaviour by cutting peaks and filling valleys. However, consider-
ng the differences in geographic locations, some buildings have
ery adequate solar conditions and excess power generated by
V, while others have poor solar conditions and PV power cannot
eet daily load demand. The imbalance in PV output may limit

he consumption of clean solar energy for building users.
Peer-to-peer (P2P) service is a decentralized platform whereby

wo individuals interact directly with each other (Fig. 1). P2P en-
rgy trading is an emerging concept in recent years, the buildings
nside the microgrid are both energy consumers and energy pro-
ucers, also named ‘‘prosumer’’, i.e., they participate in both elec-
ricity generation and consumption. For buildings with surplus
lectricity generation, they can sell electricity to neighbouring
uildings through P2P energy trading and earn some revenue.
or buildings with insufficient electricity generation, they can buy
lectricity through P2P at a price that may be lower than the price
f external grid. This results in a ‘‘friendly agreement’’ between

able to use clean energy equitably, cheaply and efficiently, in
addition, more users will be attracted to participate in the group.

1.2. Literature review

The core of P2P energy trading is the market trading mecha-
nisms. Current research on the study of P2P market mechanisms
are mainly focused on centralized, decentralized and distributed
markets (also called community-based market, full market, hy-
brid P2P market) (Zhou et al., 2020; Esteban et al., 2021). The
centralized market maximizes the overall benefits of the entire
energy trading community through a centralized management
approach. There is a market coordinator in the centralized market
who is able to regulate the operation of the entire micro-grid
from a macro perspective. The coordinator sets the objective
function and maximizes the total benefits in the group. The
decentralized does not have the market coordinator, it enables
direct trading and negotiation between users without sharing
their own energy data to a third party. Distributed market, which

The proposed solution combines the characteristics of several
market mechanisms. It requires the analysis of historical data
and user behaviour, enables indirectly influencing user behaviour
by setting price mechanisms, also allows other user to join the
microgrid at any time. This paper designs a cost sharing method
based on data envelopment analysis (DEA) theory for buildings
which want to join P2P energy trading. Considering that the PV
and ESS equipment are regarded as ‘‘one-time investment’’, an
optimal operation scheme of the P2P microgrid is also proposed,
in which buildings can buy electricity from the external grid,
use P2P energy trading within the microgrid, or use their own
generated PV power.

introduces the multiple scenarios of unsing the energy trad-
ing method. Section 5 analyses the case study and Section 6
concludes this paper.

2. Optimal capacity allocation and scheduling of microgrid
(1st layer of optimization)

The optimal capacity allocation and scheduling of building
microgrid is the first layer of optimization model. When multiple
buildings combine to form a microgrid, in order to maximize
energy utilization, a combined operation scheme needs to be
formed at first. The microgrid should trade as little electricity as
possible with the external grid and consume its own generated
electricity as much as possible.

. Probable scenarios and suggested solutions

In practice, there may exist 3 cases (Fig. 2).
• Case 1 — Several buildings plan to raise funds to install PV

and ESS equipment together, and every building is suitable
to install PV modules for self-generation (All buildings can
provide clean energy for the microgrid, that is, all buildings
are prosumers).

• Solution 1 — In this case, the solution has 3 steps. 1st:
the load data of each building is obtained and the area of
the installed PV modules are firstly measured. 2nd:Using
the optimization method of Section 2 to calculate the ca-
pacity allocation and optimal operation method for each
building. 3rd: Obtaining the actual cost sharing according to
Section 3(all buildings in the orange square of Fig. 2(a)).

• Case 2 — Several buildings plan to raise funds to install
PV and ESS equipment together, while some buildings are
not suitable to install PV modules (some buildings are un-
able to provide energy for the microgrid, that are totally
consumers).

• Solution 2 — Different from case 1, the initial conditions
for buildings which cannot install PV modules need to be
modified during the optimization of Section 2. However,
these consumers still need to be considered in the capac-
ity allocation and are also involved in the subsequent cost
sharing (in the orange square in Fig. 2(b)). The solution
has 3 steps. 1st: Modify initial conditions according to the
actual installation (the building which cannot provide PV
energy should be considered). 2nd: Using the optimization
method of Section 2 to calculate the capacity allocation and
optimal operation method for each building. 3rd: Obtain
the actual cost sharing according to Section 3(the building
which cannot provide PV energy should be considered).

• Case 3 — Several buildings has agreed to share their re-
sources (PV and ESS), while other consumers want to join
this microgrid after the cost sharing has reached agreement.

• Solution 3 — Case 3 is actually a further study based on
case 1. Here solution 3 is summarized in the following 2
steps. 1st: Define the optimal operation and cost sharing
strategy for the original building group (refer to solution
1). 2nd: When other buildings participate in, the optimal
operation should be recalculated (the cost sharing strategy
remains the same). The newly joined building should use the
dynamic pricing mechanism (Section 4) to trade with other
users.

7. Conclusion

This paper proposes a P2P energy trading method for build-
ing microgrid based on DEA. By applying the proposed strategy,
buildings in the microgrid are not only able to share the self-
generated clean energy in a more reasonable way, but also ob-
tain appreciable economic benefits. Furthermore, attracting more
users to participate in the P2P energy trading, forming a virtuous
cycle of green carbon reduction.

This method enables the allocation of costs to the microgrid
in different practical application scenarios, and determine the
P2P dynamic pricing mechanism based on the current electricity
price of the external grid. The results are compared under three
different cases where all buildings are prosumers (solution 1),
some buildings are unable to provide energy for the microgrid
(solution 2), and some buildings want to participate in the micro-
grid after the cost sharing is agreed (solution 3). Taking B5 as the
typical example (B5 is considered as the ’independent variable’),
B5 could save 89%, 59% and 35% of electricity costs under solution
1,solution 2, solution 3, respectively. Further analysis introduces
the comparison of B1∼B4 in the three solutions. The results show
that even if the buildings are equipped with costly new energy
devices, the DEA cost sharing method and the internal trading
method proposed in this paper can still achieve a reasonable
distribution of electricity consumption. In addition, each user
could benefits from the proposed strategy, not only contributing
to the green carbon reduction concept but also saving more costs.
