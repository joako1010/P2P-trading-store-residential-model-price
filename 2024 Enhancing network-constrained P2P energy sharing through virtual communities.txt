A transactive energy-sharing framework has been developed in [13],
in which the many roles of prosumers, such as buyer, seller, and
neither, are analyzed. A two-level energy market is proposed in [14],
where one level pertains to trade within the community, and the other
pertains to trade between the utility and the community controller.
However, no energy storage such as BESS is taken into account in [14].
In [15], a game theoretic model is proposed consisting of a non-
cooperative game and an evolutionary game. Sellers play the former
game for the price, and buyers play the latter to select the seller. For
each time slot, the buyers and sellers are pre-defined in the model
proposed in [16] and use the social welfare of buyers and sellers as
the objective for optimization. In [17], a Stackelberg game is proposed
with sellers at the upper level for the pricing problem and buyers at the
lower level for purchasing problem. A pair-matching algorithm is used
in [18] for matching the respective buyer and seller. In [17], energy
storage systems and demand response are not considered, which does
not represent a practical scenario. Based on the available excess energy,
these works [13вҖ“18], at the beginning itself, categorize the participants
as a seller or a buyer. However, the BESS, flexible load shifting, and
the strategies of other participants may encourage the participants
to switch roles. Thus, the participants should have the autonomy in
selecting their roles of either a buyer or seller or both or none.

The network parameters, like voltages, power flow, power loss, etc.,
get affected by P2P energy trading. Bus voltage and line loading may vi-
olate their limit if the network constraints are not considered. This will
result in increased power loss and reduced power quality. This is one
of the important challenges faced in peer-to-peer energy trading [19].
For local resilience, load balancing, and facilitating P2P trading, the
distribution system operator (DSO) has to work out certain arrange-
ments [20]. In [21], DSO ensures the feasibility of P2P energy trading
by changing the network switchвҖҷs state and maintaining the network
parameters within limits. Along with buyers and sellers, a profit-based
DSO equipped with Volt-Var control resources also participates as a
player in the energy market [22]. In [22], the cooperative game-
based optimization model includes network parameters and related
constraints in an unfeasible approach because DSOs have refrained
from sharing this information due to security concerns. This issue is
resolved by using network usage charges in P2P problems [23,24],
instead of using network constraints directly. But these charges are
considered as a pre-fixed parameter in these works that give inadequate
control to DSO over the violation level. However, dynamic network us-
age charges would give DSO proper control to fine-tune the transactions
occurring through the network.

The sensitivity analysis can also be used to prevent the violation
of network constraints [25]. An iterative algorithm to maintain the
network parameter in limits under P2P energy sharing has been pro-
posed in [26]. Any voltage or current limit violation is penalized by
a certain fee, which is considered for re-scheduling P2P sharing. This
process continues till the network parameter falls under the limits.
However, in [25,26], the network usage charges are not considered. For
the market operation, through the optimal power flow (OPF), the DSO
in [27] calculates the locational marginal price (LMP) that accounts
for voltage regulation and power loss. But privacy implications arise
when employing a centralized approach in [27]. Also, [27] does not
consider the energy storage system. For investigating the power bal-
ancing and pricing mechanisms in P2P energy exchange, [28] proposes
a linear LinDistFlow model. In such optimum scheduling, incorporating
renewable power sources may result in some uncertainties that are
neglected in [28]. These uncertainties create a mismatch between local
supply and demand, leading to system unreliability and instability [29].
In [30], the proposed framework enables trading within the community

and neighboring areas, facilitated by a community manager that is

The P2P energy sharing involves the incentive/price distribution
also that encourages the participants to engage in such internal trading.
Based on the supply and demand ratio (SDR), [31] proposes a dynamic
tariff framework for P2P trading that is limited by the feed-in tariffs.

Ref. [33,34] propose methods for incentive distribution based on the
mid-market rate. For these methods, the participants need to share their
energy profiles raising privacy concerns. Also, they also do not ensure
suitable economic satisfaction for each of the participants without
compromising their privacy. The authors [35] have examined hourly
pricing mechanisms and incentive mechanisms and found that both
mechanisms produce almost identical results. The main difference is
in the execution time of both mechanisms. The incentive mechanism is
comparatively faster.

The review of aforementioned works on P2P energy trading has
highlighted specific areas where further research is needed. Technically
infeasible P2P energy transactions may result if network constraints are
ignored. In the existing literature, these constraints have not been ex-
tensively explored; instead, other approaches have been adopted. These
approaches include using pre-fixed network usage charges that have
the problem of inadequate control on energy transactions in regard to
network constraints [23,24]; or including these constraints explicitly
in the problem formulation [22] that will lead to security issues as it
needed DSO to share data with the participants; or involving a third-
party agent [30] that can lead to privacy issues. Also, in the existing
work [13вҖ“17], participants are classified into the roles of buyers and
sellers at the beginning itself, but they should have the autonomy to
switch their roles as seller or buyer depending on the market scenario
and other participants strategies. These research gaps related to the
feasibility of network constraints and participantsвҖҷ privacy participants
privacy are addressed in this work.

The proposed decentralized P2P framework fine-tunes the trans-
actions regarding the technical constraints, leveraging the benefits of
dynamic network usage charges and electricity tariffs. At its core,
this frameworkвҖҷs dedicated commitment is to prevent inadvertent data
exposure across the complex communications web between participants
and distribution system operators (DSOs). To ensure security, cloud
computing infrastructures are strategically utilized to enable efficient
communication with the DSO, specifically in the context of net power
supply or absorption at specified bus locations. It is paramount to
recognize the important role of cloud computing in orchestrating peer-
to-peer (P2P) energy sharing, extending its impact to intra-bus and
inter-bus domains. This forward-thinking approach leverages the con-
cept of virtual communities to manage and optimize the complicated
dynamics of energy sharing efficiently. In the proposed P2P energy-
sharing framework, the intrinsic responsibilities and dynamics of the
prosumers are determined through an endogenous process, effectively
removing the need for prior knowledge concerning their deficit or
surplus energy status.

Furthermore, this study considers the uncertainties in renewable
energy generation, thus reinforcing its commitment to realism and
practical applicability. In the face of a progressively expanding envi-
ronment, the burden carried by the communication infrastructure is
undeniably enhanced, resulting in a simultaneous increase in compu-
tational complexities [36]. Also, for communication with DSO, either
each building does it directly or an aggregator at each bus is present for
this communication in the existing literature. The diverse challenges
explored in this work are methodically resolved by harnessing the
capabilities of virtual communities (VCs), which are the hypothetical
gathering of all the buildings of each bus. This methodical framework
effectively diminishes the burden of transactions without compromis-
ing the integrity of peer-to-peer (P2P) energy sharing, thereby giving
rise to a scalable P2P energy-sharing infrastructure. The conventional

practice of distributing Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) within l

3

individual building units is replaced by a more coordinated approach,
whereby a designated group of buildings shares BESS. The concept of
virtual communities facilitates the orchestration of this collaborative
configuration.

2. Problem formulation

2.1. System model

In this work, all the neighboring buildings and BESS on a single bus
(Fig. 1) are grouped to form virtual communities (VCs). Here, VC is
nothing but a hypothetical gathering of shareable BESS and buildings
interconnected through a computing cloud. Smart metering and a
robust communication system are assumed to be present to facilitate
the buildings equipped with demand-responsive loads and RESs. For
cloud computing and limited information sharing, a separate and secure
platform is present for each internal energy trading (i.e. building-
to-building (B2B), building-to-community (B2C), and community-to-
community (C2C)). In each VC, cloud computing is used to handle the
coupling decisions of buildings and BESS that are required for B2B and
B2C trading, charging/discharging of BESS, and trading with other VCs
and the utility. The VC considered here is a non-profit based for its
buildings and profit based with respect to other VCs for C2C trading.
This concept takes advantage of P2P sharing among buildings while
minimizing the need for extensive communication and computational
resources. It aims to enhance the overall benefits of the buildings.
In the process of this internal P2P trading, the network parameters
may violate the network constraints. An independent operator, DSO,
ensures that all the network parameters are regulated within acceptable
thresholds with the help of a dynamic network utilization cost (NUC).

The complete steps of energy scheduling are presented in the
flowchart shown in Fig. 2. The рқңҶрқ‘ғ and рқңҶрқҗ· refer to Primal and dual resid-
uals, and (рқң…, рқңҲ) are given constants. After initialization, each player at
the building level will simultaneously optimize its strategies, and then
inside each VC, the auxiliary variables will be optimized for B2B energy
trade. After this, we reach to VC level for the optimization of auxiliary
variables for B2C energy trade and the objective of VCs. In the end,
the auxiliary variables for C2C are optimized. After each optimization
of auxiliary variables (рқңҺ), the dual multipliers (рқң”) are updated. Based
on the primal and dual residuals (рқңҶрқ‘ғ&рқңҶрқҗ·), the penalty parameters (рқӣҝ)
are updated. This whole process is repeated until the tolerance for
dual multipliers (рқң”) has been reached. After the calculation of all these
power profiles, net power injection is communicated to DSO and the
LMPs (34) are calculated using the DSO level problem 2.4. The new
LMPs are broadcast, based on which the VCs reschedule their power
profiles. This process of updating LMPs and power profiles is repeated
until the convergence, i.e., the difference between the LMPs of two
consecutive iterations becomes less than a tolerance threshold. Apart
from the flowchart shown in Fig. 2, a schematic view of the proposed
model is also present in Fig. 3. This diagram depicts the various inputs
this model needs to get the various outputs as shown in it. The ultimate
output is economic savings for all without violating network constraints
and maintaining the privacy of each participant.
