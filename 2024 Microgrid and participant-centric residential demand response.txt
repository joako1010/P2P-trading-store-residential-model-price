Keywords: An innovative decentralized Residential Demand Response (DR) program offers an effective solution for efficient 
Energy management energy management to save cost for both the participants and the community Microgrid (MG) and facilitate peer- 
Energy trading to-peer (P2P) trading of excess energy of photovoltaic (PV) with battery energy storage system (BESS) between 
Demand response (DR) prosumers and consumers residing within a community. This study aims to optimally schedule home appliances, 
Peer-to-peer (P2P) 
Demand reduction bid (DRB) mitigate the detrimental externality effect on tariff rates among participants, select participants for demand 
Direct load control (DLC) reduction, select prosumers for buying energy in P2P energy trading, and protect the privacy of participants. This 

study aims to optimally schedule home appliances, mitigate the detrimental externality effect on tariff rates 
among participants, select participants for demand reduction, select prosumers for buying energy in P2P energy 
trading, and protect the privacy of participants. This novel approach combines the advantages of demand 
response under the multi-time of use (TOU) pricing and the incentive-driven demand reduction bidding mech
anism under direct load control (DLC) to reduce electricity demand, minimize electricity costs for enrolled 
participants, and save cost for the community MG by offering a minimum incentive to participants. The inte
gration of the Home Energy Management System (HEMS) and Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI) enables a 
dynamic and responsive energy management system, fostering a more active role for participants in optimizing 
their individual energy usage patterns and incentivizing participants to adjust their energy consumption based on 
a demand reduction bidding strategy. The detrimental externality effect on tariff rates is managed among par
ticipants with varying utilization levels through a multi-dynamic utilization level pricing scheme (MDULPS). This 
study utilizes a novel non-cooperative game design strategy, incorporating a Nash equilibrium approach to 
achieve the optimum schedule of smart home appliances and the selection of participants for demand reduction 
with the lowest possible demand reduction bids. The economically selection of preferred prosumer for buying 
energy considering consumer utility in P2P energy trading scheme is accomplished using Prosumer Preference 
Based Segmented Distributed Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (PPBSD-ADMM). The proposed ap
proaches for energy management and energy trading are more feasible in terms of preserving participants' 
privacy compared to other state-of-the-art strategies. Finally, analytical and simulation results show that the 
proposed demand response and P2P energy trading schemes are suitable and effective and have benefits with 
regards to prosumer cost savings of up to 54.55 %, consumer cost savings of up to 33.28 %, and MG cost savings 
of up to 34.65 %.

6. Simulations results and discussion 

6.1. Selection of participants for demand reduction according to DRB 

The Direct Load Control (DLC) strategy is proposed to a compre
hensive evaluation for demand reduction within the framework of a 
power system comprising ten participants. This strategy aims to reduce 
the MG's costs by providing minimal incentives to participants for de
mand reduction during various time intervals. Participants establish and 
submit their incentive bid prices for demand reduction to the MG. Data 
regarding these submitted incentive bid prices from all levels of par
ticipants are presented in Table 2. The MG evaluates these bids, and 
creates the stages for combining participants from different levels for 
demand reduction, as detailed in Section 5.2. However, the MG selects 
participants offering the lowest incentive bid prices for demand reduc
tion, thus optimizing its cost savings. The bids generally incorporate 
both the requested price at a specific time and the available capacity for 
demand reduction. This study motivates participants to suggest load 
reductions at prices they consider suitable for potential curtailment. The 
reduction in demand from various participants is shown in Fig. 3. 

6.1.1. Case study 
In case 1, participants 1, 6, and 7 are evaluated as top-priority par

ticipants for demand reduction across all CPs levels, owing to their 
lowest bids prices submitted to the MG. Conversely, participants 3, 5, 
and 10 are identified as the lowest priority, attributed to their submis
sion of the highest bids prices for demand reduction. In case 2, partici
pant 1 registers a 20 % increase in their bid rate, resulting in their 
reclassification to a stage level 2 participant. In case 3, participant 3 
increased its bids prices by 10 %, which results in classified as stage level 
3 participant. In case 4, participant 3 reduced their bids prices by 25 %, 
which classify them as a stage level 1 participant as shown in Fig. 3. 

Table 2 illustrates the incentive rate as a bid price that participants 
submit to the MG for six different phases of the day. Participants adjust 
their demand based on the overall demand of cross-elastic load, the 
group level of MDULPS, and the stage level determined by cumulative 
reduction bids. The algorithm encompasses a cumulative power reduc
tion range from 1 kW to 61.976 kW, achieved by all participants across 
four distinct cases. This range is attributed to the potential scenario 
where all participants are engaged in power curtailment, enabling the 
algorithm to achieve a collective total load reduction of up to 61.976 kW 
within a specific interval. After the execution of the algorithm, MG 
suggests the exact power potential to the participants that need to be 
curtailed for every potential CP level within the specified time period. 
The outcomes and a detailed discussion of these four cases are presented 
in Appendix A.
